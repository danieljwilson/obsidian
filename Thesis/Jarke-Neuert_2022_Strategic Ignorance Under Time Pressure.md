# Notes
## Summary
>[!Quote] 
>we hypothesize that time pressure elevates the prevalence of strategic ignorance

👆Are other ways to say this, with slightly different implications not:
- time pressure means you have time to accumulate less information
- time pressure moves you from optimizing to satisficing (if you were really trying to optimize in the first place...optimizing is hard so if the delta between optimal and satisficing which may use some simple heuristic needs to be meaningful)

Not sure I agree with the statement:
>Information avoidance is particularly common in situations where privately beneficial choices have uncertain effects on others

 👆 I think that what we don't like is our own personal negative affect...if we negatively affect others it feels bad...if we negatively affect ourselves it feels bad (avoiding checking bank account balance); idea of its better to ask forgiveness than permission...

They say that 
>information avoidance under time pressure can be attributable to conflict avoidance

👆 But is it really avoidance if you just don't have time? ==Information prioritization== just doesn't have an effect under a no time pressure condition...in some respects it is more a case of "avoidance" if you don't look during the NO time pressure condition. No external pressure not to look...
- they do say a follow up experiment suggested that decision makers "cannot deal with more information" under time pressure, but not sure what "deal" means in this context...

Also not sure if I agree with the idea of "costless" information - takes energy and time to process information even if you are not being charged an objective fee...

Real "moral" conflict in the lab situation seems like a stretch.
- The monetary amounts are extremely small - not very high stakes

**Policy examples not clear how they connect to time pressure?**
- consumers tend to ignore ethically relevant product information even when it is easily accessible.
- information avoidance in an organ transplantation context as a means of ignoring uncomfortable moral conflicts
- information avoidance to insulate against liability

One version of optimal behavior would just be to choose the higher amount for yourself each time. This would optimize for highest total payment from researchers -> subjects...could also optimize for fairness

Also think that the use average time is just poor design - do it by person!




## Thoughts
>According to a recent poll of 2,000 people, 55 percent admit fear keeps them from monitoring their bank account as regularly as they should. [Link](https://www.studyfinds.org/atm-anxiety-people-fear-checking-bank-money-balance/)

We avoid info that provides negative affect and no real benefit...it's not going to change anything by looking at my portfolio on days that the stock market is down (nor when it is up) but the affective outcome will be different.
