## Paper
[[Gigerenzer_G_2022_Smart Heuristics for Individuals, Teams and Organizations]]


>[!Quote]
Heuristics are fast, frugal, and accurate strategies that enable rather than limit decision making under uncertainty.

>Uncertainty, as opposed to calculable risk, is characteristic of most organizational contexts.

👆This seems like a tricky definition. 
- Even with calculable risk there is "uncertainty"
	- I can calculate the exact likelihood of rolling a 6 but that is independent of the fact that when I roll the die I am uncertain about the outcome.
- And it is not a binary - some risks/odds are less precisely known than others...but that does not make them in-calculable, it just makes them estimates with wider error bars

Research on ==adaptive toolbox== **descriptive**
Research on ==ecological rationality== is **prescriptive**

In some ways heuristic just seems relative, not categorical. A simpler version of an algorithm = a heuristic

Are heuristics becoming out of date - as AI models get more powerful can we not add that to our adaptive toolbox? And isn't this able to perform the computationally infeasible rational strategies such as expected utility maximization?

>[!Quote]
>In a situation of risk, an agent has perfect knowledge of all relevant future states of the world and their probabilities and consequences.

>[!Important]
>The general point is that probability theory and optimization
models are perfect tools for risk and that heuristics are the better tools for uncertainty.

👆I just don't think that it makes sense to totally separate these completely.

Aren't you optimizing across heuristics? And isn't that optimization based on the probability of the heuristics being correct?

They define [[risk]] as:
> a situation in which all possible events, probabilities, and consequences are known and optimal action can be calculated (e.g., lotteries, roulette)

They define [[uncertainty]] as:
>situations in which perfect foresight of all future events, their consequences, and probabilities is impossible (e.g., hiring, project selection); also known as VUCA environments
