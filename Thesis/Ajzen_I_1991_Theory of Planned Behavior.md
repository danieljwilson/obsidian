# Notes
## Summary
**Motivating Idea** 
Intentions (to perform behaviors) can be accurately predicted from:
1. ==attitudes (toward the behavior)==
	- the degree to which a person has a favorable or unfavorable evaluation or appraisal of the behavior in question
2. ==subjective norms==
	- refers to the perceived social pressure to perform or not to perform the behavior
3. ==perceived behavioral control==^[This was added from the previous [[Theory of Reasoned Action]]]
	- perceived ease or difficulty of performing the behavior and it is assumed to reflect past experience as well as anticipated impediments and obstacles
	- has to do with presence or absence of necessary "resources" and opportunities
	- specific to the particular behavior/action (i.e. not the same as [[locus of control]])
	- how easy/effortful is it to achieve the intention?
	- has similarities to [[Albert Bandura]]'s [[self-efficacy]] construct

The importance of each of these three components is not fixed and varies according to the situation.

Points out that generally attitudes/personality traits tend to be poor predictor of behavior (as alluded to by [[Wicker_AW_1969_Attitudes versus Actions - The Relationship of Verbal and Overt Behavioral Responses to Attitude Objects]]). 

- One way of improving predictive validity is to aggregate over different behaviors on different occasions
>general attitudes and personality traits doin fact predict behavioral aggregates much better than they predict specific behaviors (Ajzen, 1988)
- However this doesn't solve the problem of predicting behavior in specific situations or explaining the variability in behavior.

**This footnote indicates how the model equates intention and behavior**^[I think this is a major flaw...as we know that they are NOT synonymous]
>The original derivation of the theory of planned behavior (Aizen, 1985) defined intention (and its other theoretical constructs) in terms of trying to perform a given behavior rather than in relation to actual performance. However, early work with the model showed strong correlations between measures of the model's variables that asked about trying to perform a given behavior and measures that dealt with actual performance of the behavior (Schifter & Ajzen, 1985; Ajzen & Madden, 1986).Since the latter measures are less cumbersome, they have been used in subsequent research, and the variables are now defined more simply in relation to behavioral performance.

Important point is that **perceived behavioral control may be wrong**!
- **domain knowledge**: subject has little experience/information re: the intention domain
- **context change**: requirements or available resources have changed, or new and unfamiliar elements have entered the situation

**Ajzen believes that**:
>As a general rule it is found that when behaviors pose no serious problems of control, they can be predicted from intentions with considerable accuracy (see Ajzen, 1988; Sheppard, Hartwick, & Warshaw, 1988).

In a series of studies using the theory of planned behavior they found that attitudes and perceived behavioral control basically always were significant predictors of intention but that subjective norms were a mixed bag.^[This seems like it could be a case of individual differences...some people more susceptible to social pressure than others...although I guess you would still expect a main effect?]

**Ajzen says that his theory explains human behavior, not just predicts it...**
Claims that it deals with the "antecedents of attitudes, subjective norms and pbc..."
- behavior is a function of ==salient information==, or beliefs, relevant to the behavior
	- behavioral beliefs
	- normative beliefs
	- control beliefs

**Discuss [[expectancy-value model of attitude]]**
- relationship should exist between salient beliefs and attitude toward and object/action^["although the magnitude of this relation has sometimes been disappointing"...so funny that they are actively admitting that they are rooting for a certain outcome...huge red flag in my mind]

**Touch on [[habitual behavior]]**
- can reduce the mediation of attitudes, subjective norms and perceptions of control (or intentions) in predicting behavior
- past behavior does not necessarily imply [[habitual behavior|habit]]...as each behavior could have arisen independently on each occasion from a stable set of attitudes/subjective norms/perceptions of control
- past behavior is a good indicator of behavioral control...
 
**Intentions + Perceptions of behavioral control**
At the end of the paper talk about move to [[theory of planned behavior]] from [[Theory of Reasoned Action]] and point out that behavior prediction was often greatly increased by including not just intentions but also perceptions of behavioral control.
- similar to my idea of $ability * intention$ but broader in that ability could include both perceptions of behavioral control (e.g. self-efficacy) and actual capacity (e.g. IQ)

## Thoughts
Focus seems to be on explaining behavior (rather than just predicting)...idea seems to also be though that if you can explain it your can predict it...

I would argue that [[behavior]] is jointly determined by more factors than just **[[intention]]** and **perceived behavioral control**...
- ability
- planning (which may also have an ability component)
- external factors
Perhaps the focus should be on "actual" behavioral control. I think I refer to this as ability. Ability combines with intention to predict behavior...

I'm not sure that just because a behavior "poses no serious problem of control" that it can be predicted just from intentions. Or I think that the idea of control contains a lot. Something can be easy and still hard to do...going to bed early...

When he states:
>A number of investigators have begun to rely on the theory of planned behavior in their attempts to predict and understand people's intentions to engage in various activities.

I wonder if they are actually predicting intentions or behaviors in these studies?
