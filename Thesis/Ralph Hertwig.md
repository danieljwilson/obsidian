# Notes
>Known for his work in the fields of judgment, decision-making, and risk. He's especially renowned for his research on bounded rationality, a concept that suggests people make decisions based on the information they have, which is usually limited, as opposed to making decisions based on all possible information.
>
>Hertwig's work often involves experimental psychology and has largely contributed to behavioral economics. He has studied a broad range of topics within these fields, including the exploration of heuristics (mental shortcuts), risk literacy, medical decision-making, and the ways in which people use and understand statistical information.
>
>One of Hertwig's most well-known contributions is the concept of "fast and frugal heuristics". Alongside [[Gerd Gigerenzer]], another influential figure in the field, Hertwig proposed that in many real-world situations, simpler decision-making tools can be more effective than complex ones. This has been a significant paradigm shift, challenging the traditional economic models that assume fully rational actors with complete information.
>
>He's also known for his work on "experience-based decision making," in which he highlighted that decisions made based on personal experience can often be quite different from those made based on described information.
>
>Hertwig has served as the director of the Center for Adaptive Rationality at the Max Planck Institute for Human Development in Berlin, where he has led a number of important research projects. His work has greatly contributed to our understanding of human cognition and decision-making, providing insights not just for psychology, but also for fields such as economics, health, and public policy.
