# Notes

## ChatGPT + Science Summary
The paper explores the paradoxical relationship between the robustness of cognitive tasks and the reliability of individual differences in cognitive paradigms. Despite the common use of individual differences in cognitive paradigms to relate cognition to brain structure, chemistry, and function, these efforts often prove unfruitful, even with well-established tasks.

The authors propose an explanation for this paradox, stating that ==experimental effects become robust when between-subject variability is low==. This low variability, in turn, leads to low reliability in individual differences, destroying replicable correlations with other factors and potentially undermining published conclusions drawn from correlational relationships.

The paper includes three studies that assessed the test-retest reliability of seven classic cognitive tasks: Eriksen Flanker, Stroop, stop-signal, go/no-go, Posner cueing, Navon, and Spatial-Numerical Association of Response Code (SNARC). The reliabilities ranged from 0 to 0.82, surprisingly low given their common use.

==The authors found that the paradox emerged from low variance between individuals rather than high measurement variance==. In other words, the very reason these tasks produce easily replicable experimental effects between participants makes their use as tools for studying individual differences problematic.

The paper concludes by demonstrating that taking these reliability estimates into account has the potential to qualitatively change theoretical conclusions. ==The findings imply that well-established approaches in neuropsychology may not directly translate to individual cognitive function, and alternative metrics may be required.==

**Different meanings of reliability**
==Experiments==
A reliable effect is one that nearly always replicates, one that is shown by most participants in any study and produces consistent effect sizes. (e.g. Stroop effect almost always replicates)
==Correlational Research==
Reliability refers to the extent to which a measure consistently *ranks individuals*.

In other words, "==the reliability of two measures limits the correlation that can be observed between them (Nunnally, 1970; Spearman, 1904)=="
