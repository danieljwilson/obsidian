## Chat GPT Summary
Harry Frankfurt's "Freedom of the Will and the Concept of a Person" is a seminal paper in the philosophy of mind and action. Frankfurt aims to **clarify the concepts of personhood and free will**, and their interrelationship.

1. **Concept of a Person**: Frankfurt begins by distinguishing between persons and other creatures. Persons, according to Frankfurt, are beings with ==reflective minds==, meaning ==they can form desires about desires==. That is, they are capable of second-order desires, which are desires about having or not having certain first-order desires. For example, a person may not only desire to eat chocolate (a first-order desire), but also desire to have the desire to eat chocolate (a second-order desire). ==Non-persons, on the other hand, are beings that can only have first-order desires.==
    
2. **Structure of the Will**: Frankfurt argues that having second-order desires is crucial for the structure of the will. ==A person's will is determined by their second-order volitions==, which are second-order desires that one wants to be their will. For example, a person may have a second-order desire to quit smoking, but this becomes a second-order volition only when the person wants this desire to be effective in guiding their actions.
    
3. **Free Will**: According to Frankfurt, ==an individual has free will when their second-order volitions are in harmony with their first-order desires==.^[This is a freedom from the tyranny of your first order desires. Though tyranny is probably the wrong way to think about it...isn't it also a certain form of freedom to indulge your first order desires?? For example if you are rich then you have much more of an opportunity to do that...isn't that a form of freedom?] That is, a person is free when they want a certain desire to be their will, and this desire effectively governs their actions. Conversely, a person is unfree when there is a conflict between their second-order volitions and their first-order desires.
    
4. **Moral Responsibility**: Frankfurt contends that moral responsibility hinges on the presence of second-order volitions. ==A person is morally responsible for their actions when they act according to their second-order volitions==. If a person acts on a first-order desire without it being endorsed by a second-order volition, they are not morally responsible for that action^[This seems weird. In summary, Frankfurt's paper provides a nuanced analysis of **personhood**, **free will**, and **moral responsibility** by focusing on the structure of the will and the role of second-order desires and volitions. His account has been influential in shaping subsequent discussions on these topics.
