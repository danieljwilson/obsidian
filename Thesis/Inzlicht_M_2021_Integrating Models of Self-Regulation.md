## Summary
[[self-regulation|Self-regulation]] is ==a core aspect of human functioning that helps facilitate the successful pursuit of personal goals==.

**Problem**
>progress in understanding self-regulation has been hampered by too many models and theories, each describing self-regulation at different levels of analysis with not enough integration.

#### Definitions
[[self-regulation|Self-regulation]], [[self control|self-control]] and [[cognitive control]] are often used interchangeably...

They suggest:
- [[Self-regulation]] is abroad term that refers to the dynamic process of determining a desired end state (i.e., a goal)^[this seems like extra ground to claim - that the goal setting itself involves self-regulation? Could it not require more self-regulation to set reasonable goals as opposed to unrealistic goals? In that case it is not the goal setting a priori that involves self-regulation] and then taking action to move toward it while monitoring progress along the way (Carver & Scheier 1998)
- **conflict** between motives differentiates [[self control|self-control]] from [[self-regulation]] (Fujita 2011).
- [[self-regulation|Self-regulation]] can be conflict free
- [[cognitive control|Cognitive control]] is the flexible allocation of attention in the service of goal-directed behavior in the face of more habitual or immediately compelling behaviors (Cohen 2017)

## Thoughts
A bit of an issue with the definition of self control:
> The process of advancing one goal over a second goal when the two come into conflict.

What if the two goals are partying with friends and studying for an exam. Is it self control to advance the goal of partying with friends? I believe the goal needs to be aligned with [[second-order preference]] to require self control.

Do the models put forward all describe *themselves* as models of [[self-regulation]]? It seems like for [[Arie W. Kruglanski]]'s Goal Theory and Wiener's [[Cybernetic control]] that there is an explicit focus on goals, and how those are accomplished.

- Cybernetic theory kind of like a reinforcement learning model. 
- Goal systems theory speaks to how the implementing system chooses actions
- Dual Systems speak to how our [[first-order preference]] and [[second-order preference]] can bias our **goals**, **conflict monitoring**, and **implementation system**.
- [[Choice models]] seem like they are not in conflict with [[dual-process model]] - just that these two systems are involved in the choice process (and can bias it)...can affect setting of **goals** and **choice of plans**


Perhaps **competition** is a *bigger* concept than **conflict**? Things can be in competition (running a 100m race), but not in "conflict" (e.g. a tug of war battle). You can have goals that are in competition, but not conflict (i.e. they are aligned), or goals that are in competition **and** conflict (i.e. they are unaligned and pursuing one impedes progress on the other)

Do [[dual-process model]]s of control necessarily start with conflict? What if both the habitual and control systems are in agreement? What if there is conflict within the higher level [[system 2]] (i.e. multiple conflicting goals that are all aligned with your [[second-order preference]])?
