# Notes
## Summary
>[!Note]
>Aim is to outline the **mechanisms underlying habitual control** and describe the ways in which **habits interface with goals**


**Starting point -> goals can:**
1. direct habits by ==motivating repetition==  that  ==leads  to  habit  formation== by  promoting  exposure  to  cues  that  trigger  habits be inferred from habits^[create habits]
2. be inferred from habits^[specifically our "good" habits]
3. interact with habits in ways that preserve the learned habit associations

Habits are connected to the fact that our life involves routines/patterns that are **repeated**...(based on space/time)

**Context can trigger habit**
- encoding of context–response patterns in procedural memory

>[!Quote]
>Habits typically  are  the  residue  of  past  goal  pursuit

>Understanding of  the  habit–goal  interface  thus  echoes  [[dual-process model]]s  that specify  how  automatic,  associative  processes  interact  with  more controlled,  rule-based  processes

**[[behaviorism]] -> [[cognitive science]]**
- the latter focussed on internal mental processes as the cause of behavior, especially through a central [[executive control]]ler...
- [[behaviorism]] more bottom up, and [[cognitive science]] more top down...

**The 2000s saw a synthesis of social-cognitive-behaviorist elements...**

**Make a big deal about** ==direct cuing== and ==motivated cuing== but I am having a hard time really seeing the difference or importance of the distinction.

Talk about [[dopamine]] as a mechanism for creating habit...creating context-response associations in memory

#### Three Principles of the Habit Model
**1. Habits are cued by context**
	1. direct cuing
	2. indirect cuing
**2. Habit context-response associations are not mediated by goals**
	1. does not depend on a goal for its performance^[this seems like a bit of a semantic distinction, as they admit that in general habits originate in goal pursuit...so their performance is promoting a goal-consistent outcome...I guess problems can arise when you change your goals and your previous habits remain...there could be a lag.]
**3. Habits interface with goals**
	1. the [[associative learning]] underlying habits is characterized by the slow, incremental accrual of information over time in procedural  memory
	2. goals promote habit learning
	3. habits inform goals (i.e. we can infer our goals from our habitual actions)
	4. habits interact with goals to guide responses


![[Pasted image 20220712110812.png]]
>[!Summary]
>Habits  are  a  form  of  slowly  accrued  automaticity  that
involves the direct association between a context and a response (**Principle 1**), so that the context can activate the response without the  mediating  involvement  of  a  goal  (**Principle  2**).  Furthermore, habit development and performance interface with the purposive dimension of mental life as represented in people’s goals (**Principle 3**).


## Thoughts
Seems like they are saying that the momentary performance of a habitual action is not in itself dependent on a "goal"...i.e. the calculation to perform the action is not taking the goal into account...though the goal was taken into account when forming the context-action association initially.

A bit of a "so what" for me...seems like a bit of an internal debate where people want to be right but it doesn't really matter that much and the differences are smaller than they seem to those doing the arguing.

Also the idea that if the goal is not represented when performing the context-action pairing then you could "overdo" the action...keep eating when you are no longer hungry for example...
