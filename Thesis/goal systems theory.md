# Notes
Two important constructs:
1. **Goals**
	Representation of desired end-states
1. **Goal-means**
	Behaviors that can help one accomplish a goal
	
- Activation of one construct (e.g. goal formation) spreads to the other constructs that are associated/connected to it (i.e. goal means).
- Activation can spread in either direction (Shah & Kruglanski, 2003)
- Strength of a connection determines the probability that a particular goal-means will be selected for goal pursuit
- Goals are often connected to multiple goal-means
- Strength of the connections is inversely related to the number of connections ^[This idea has been challenged, e.g. Bodmann, S., Hulleman, C. & Harackiewicz, J. (2008). Achievement Goal Systems: An Application of Goal Systems Theory to Achievement Goal Research. _Revue internationale de psychologie sociale_, 1(1-2), 71-96. [https://doi.org/](https://doi.org/)]


**Example**
"Do better than others" = ==Goal==
"Study for exams" = ==Goal Means==

>Consider the example of a student (Michelle) with the academic goal of “get good grades” and several goal-means that may help her accomplish this goal: “study for exams,” “review notes,” “attend every lecture,” and “attend study groups.” When Michelle thinks about her academic goal of getting good grades, she will also tend to think about the associated goal-means of studying for exams, reviewing her notes, attending lectures, and going to study groups (and she is quite unlikely to think about going to parties). Likewise, when Michelle sits down at night to study for an upcoming exam, her goal of getting good grades may also come to mind (see Figure 1).
