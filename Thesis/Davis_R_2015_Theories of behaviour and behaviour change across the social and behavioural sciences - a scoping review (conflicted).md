## Summary
>[!Note]
>Aim of paper is to identify relevant theories of behavior and behavior change for psychology, sociology, anthropology and economics

For interventions that are effective, the effects tend to be modest, with significant heterogeneity of short-term and long-term effects (Michie, Johnston, Francis, Hardeman, & Eccles, 2008).

**Importance of theory**
- understand causal mechanisms in order to target correctly
- mechanisms of action ([[mediating variable|mediators]]) can be investigated to better understand **how** an intervention brings about its effects
- summarizes the cumulative knowledge of how to change
behavior across different populations, behaviors and contexts
- theory-based interventions provide an opportunity in which theory can be tested

>[!Warning]
>There is no clear association between interventions based on theory and higher levels of success/effectiveness

Overall it is their opinion that most theories focus on individual differences (capabilities/motivation) rather than context (social and environmental variables)

- Eighty-two theories of behaviour and behaviour change were identified.

**Nine defining features were identified as conceptually important for a good theory:**
1. clarity of constructs –‘Has the case been made for the independence of constructs from each other?’
2. clarity of relationships between constructs –‘Are the relationships between constructs clearly specified?’
3. measurability –‘Is an explicit methodology for measuring the constructs given?’
4. testability –‘Has the theory been specified in such a way that it can be tested?’
5. being explanatory –‘Has the theory been used to explain/ account for a set of observations? (statistically or logically)’
6. describing causality – ‘Has the theory been used to describe mechanisms of change?’
7. achieving parsimony –‘Has the case for parsimony been made?’
8. generalisablity –‘Have generalisations been investigated across’: (a) behaviours? (b) populations? (c) contexts?’
9. having an evidence base.

>[!Important]
>Eighteen categories of behaviours were identified, with **three** accounting for 50% of the articles: ==increasing physical activity== (N = 72; 26%), ==safe sex practices== (N = 36; 13%) and smoking cessation (N = 30; 11%).

## Thoughts
The authors state:
>By using a ‘common’ or ‘favourite’ theory, rather than one that may be more suited to the particular characteristics of the target population, behaviour and context, the potential benefit of using theory is limited.

I think the problem I see with this is that it is assuming that there is in fact a correct theory out there to select for each given context. What if none of the theories are correct? And also even if one seems promising if it is unpopular and hasn't been heavily tested then you are taking a risk by using it...could just be someone with an unusual sample or p-hacking...

>to maximise effectiveness, intervention designers are likely
to benefit from drawing from a wider range of theories than currently used

👆 But do these theories EXIST??
