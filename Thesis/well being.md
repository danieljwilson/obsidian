A key index of quality of life which reflects “how people experience and evaluate their lives and specific domains and activities in their lives"^[I think that well-being might be thought of as an outcome measure/symptom of human flourishing...which is a tricky concept in itself of course. **ALT**
 The combination of feeling good and functioning well; the experience of positive emotions such as happiness and contentment as well as the development of one’s potential, having some control over one’s life, having a sense of purpose, and experiencing positive relationships.^[Huppert 2009 | A bit of a kitchen sink definition...]
 
- It is a sustainable condition that allows the individual or population to develop and thrive.
- Broader than [[subjective well-being]] as it takes into account things like health and environmental quality which you may have little or no control over...

#### MEASUREMENT
> Well-being goes beyond hedonism and the pursuit of happiness or pleasurable experience, and beyond a global evaluation (life satisfaction): it encompasses how well people are functioning, known as eudaimonic, or psychological well-being. Assessing well-being using a single subjective item approach fails to offer any insight into how people experience the aspects of their life that are fundamental to critical outcomes. An informative measure of well-being must encompass all the major components of well-being, both hedonic and eudaimonic aspects [[2](https://hqlo.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12955-020-01423-y#ref-CR2 "Allin P, Hand DJ. New statistics for old?—measuring the wellbeing of the UK. J Royal Stat Soc Ser A. 2017;180(1):3–43.")], and cannot be simplified to a unitary item of income, life satisfaction, or happiness.

#### IMPORTANCE
> Individuals with higher subjective well-being display lower mortality rates (Chida and Steptoe, 2008; Steptoe et al.,2015) and have a lower risk of disease (Davidson et al., 2010). In the workplace, employees who report higher subjective well-being have higher productivity without loss of output quality(Oswald et al., 2015), reduced rates of absenteeism (Pelled and Xin, 1999), and are rated more positively by their supervisors (Peterson et al., 2011).
