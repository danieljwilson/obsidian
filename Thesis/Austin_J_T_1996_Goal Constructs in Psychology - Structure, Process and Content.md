# Notes
## Summary
In this review, we consider the **structural**, **process**, and **content** aspects of [[goals]] across the **cognitive**, **personality**, and **motivational** domains.

==Goal constructs== are pervasive, important, and timely. 

**Look at three domains**
1. cognitive
2. personality
3. motivation

**Look at three definitional perspectives**
1. latent
2. phenomenological
3. external

#### Goal Structure
**Goal = internally represented desired state**
- based on their interrelationships
- historically the idea of goal processes supplanted that of the behaviorist model
- [[TOTE model]], Powers's model
	- both based in [[cybernetics]]
	-  a difference between a desired state and a current state drives the organism toward reducing that difference
- goals can usually be achieved through multiple means (==equifinality==)
- sometimes a subgoal/mean can help/hinder multiple higher level goals...
- homeostatic processes not considered as "goals"
- organization of goals generally thought to be ==hierarchical==
	- e.g. in Royce and Powell's (1983) [[individuality theory]] 
		- **highest level of goal** = ==optimize personal meaning==
		- **next level** = life satisfaction, maintenance of self-image, evolution of worldview goals
- how we are doing on a higher level goal depends on how we are doing on the sub-goals that support it

##### Goal Dimensions
1. Importance/commitment
	- Gollwitzer   (1993) asserted that  goal  importance  determines  subsequent goal  commitment^[defined as how long an individual is willing to strive for a specific goal]
	- function   of the  person,  situation,  and behavior
2. Difficulty
	- estimate of probability of goal attainment
		- can be made by self or others
	- related to [[self-efficacy]]
	- personal agency beliefs (M. E. Ford, 1992) = context beliefs + capability beliefs
3. Specificity/representation
	- more specific quantitative goals vs. less specific qualitative goals
	- could this also have to do with clarity of subgoals?
	- [[implementation intention|implementation intentions]]
4. Temporal Range
	- may be  a subdimension of goal difficulty (i.e. time available or required to complete at task)
5. Level of consciousness
	- relates to position in the goal hierarchy and accessibility
		- Idea of 3 level system (Rosch   & Lloyd, 1978; Smith & Medin,  1981)
			- Superordinate
			- Basic
			- Subordinate
		- Basic, or middle-level goals most common type of conscious goal
		- Within and between these levels goals that  are more salient,  available, or accessible  may be activated in working memory more easily than other goals.
		- Our highest   level  goals (superordinate),  operate  on  slower time scales,  and are generally not  subject to consideration as often as our basic goals...therefore they are less likely to receive attentional processes   (Newell,   1990;   Powers,   1973a)^[non-urgent, important in Eisenhower matrix] 
	- overlap between goal **importance** and consciousness
	- [[David C. McClelland]] (1990) proposed  a dual- level  motivation  process  in  which goals, like need  for  achievement (nAch), need  for power (nPow), and  need for  affiliation (nAff),   are  always  below  consciousness,  whereas  goals,  like achievement value or "produce 20 widgets," are accessible...
	- more common understanding   of goals is as dynamically conscious—shuttling  in  and  out  of  working  memory  as  required (Wyer & Srull,  1989)	
6. Connectedness/complexity
	- indexes the cognitive and behavioral linkages of a goal to other  goals,  subgoals,  and  behaviors
		- more complex goals = more linkages, therefore also more chances for conflict with other goals
	- idea of a goal map with nodes and edges (Cox and Wermuth, 1993)^[I think the tricky thing with this is that a map creates the image of there being something "there" to map...is this a meaningful way to think about behavior?]

#### Goal Processes
==The behaviors and cognitions related to striving toward multiple  goals.==

- goal processes  bridge gaps between the environment and cognition, physiology and cognition, and cognition and action.
- e.g. [[implementation intention|implementation intentions]] relate environmental cues to goal striving
- both behaviorist and cognitive versions of goal process theory
- [[Peter Gollwitzer]] suggested 4 phase goal sequence:
	1. Wishing
	2. Planning
	3. Acting
	4. Evaluating

**Two main approaches**
Linear Model (constructs grouped into antecedents of goals)
Cycles of goal establishment/attainment/revision

##### Goal Establishment
Select goal content and develop its dimensions
similar environment -> similar goals
**goal content** and **dimensions** may originate **internally** or **externally**
> The processes for establishing internally generated goals are open to debate (Bandura, 1991a; Powers, 1991)
> 
> A   top-down  approach might run  something like this: An intrinsic goal system that  monitors  the  operation  of the  action  hierarchy  detects  a chronically  unmet  higher  order  goal  in  the  action  hierarchy. Given a self-regulatory standard of no chronically unmet action hierarchy goal, the intrinsic system activates the establishment process for a new middle-level  (e.g., program  level) goal in  the action  hierarchy  (Powers,  1973a)
> 
> if  Simon (1955) is correct,  the first reasonable   strategy  (set  and  sequence  of  lower  order  goals whose  combined  results  pass a profitability  test;  Beach,  1990) is adopted.

Can ==reason forward== or ==reason backward==...in the first you have a problem you are trying to solve which leads you to "find a goal", in the second you establish a goal and then figure out how to get there from where you are...

##### Goal Striving/Monitoring
After goal selection/establishment and/or plan development we need to evaluate progress in order to support decisions about goal revision, strategy change, or goal attainment.

- feedback can be explicit or implicit in the environment
- Top-down vs bottom up approaches...
	- top-down needs to be supplemented by bottom-up when data pertaining to the pursuit of goals create current states that indicate discrepancies.
- Discrepancies in intrinsic and emotional subsystems often lead to changes in the goals one pursues
- habit errors vs. thought errors

##### Goal attainment/revision/persistence decisions
Disengagement  from  striving is one consequence of non-attainment  of  goals  (cf.  Scheier  &  Carver,  1988)

##### Affect
Researches believe emotions arise from:
1. Degree of goal attainment
2. Estimates of the rate of progress toward goal attainment
3. Anticipated goal attainment
4. "flow" experienced during goal striving
5. activated current concerns

- common precursor to emotional experience is [[goal conflict]]

##### Summary
- **Establishing**, **planning**, **striving**, and  **deciding**  comprise  a set of dynamic processes that are difficult to differentiate  over time.
- Individuals strive  to achieve their goals, which may be established  from  external  sources,  through  the  production  of subgoals and actions.
- Self-perceptions,   future time  orientation, and environmental  beliefs play an important role in the establishment of goals with specific properties  and levels of commitment.
- These  processes  also  lead  to  and help regulate  emotions and  internal  conflict  among  goals.

#### Goal Content
**CLASSIC TAXONOMIES** 
- William  James. McDougall (1923) proposed  12 instincts that  he  felt  directed  human  behavior.
- Maslow (1954) developed a list of needs argued to be hierarchically arranged  and to exhibit prepotency ([[Maslow's hierarchy of needs]])
	- 5 Classes: physiological, safety,  social,  esteem,  self-actualization
- Empirically inclined  researchers  factor  analyzed  ratings  of goal  lists  (e.g., Alderfer, 1969), narrowing the categories to existence, relatedness, and growth goals.
- Others  focused attention  on one or two of  Murray's  needs—nAch,  nPow,  nAff  (D.  C.  McClelland, 1965), competence  (White,  1959), and self-determination (de Charms,  1968; Deci& Ryan, 1980,1985,1991), among others.

**RECENT TAXONOMIES**
- Factor analysis  of valence, conflict, and attainment ratings of goal statements suggested six factors (Wicker et al., 1984)
	- Interpersonal concern
	- Competitive advantage
	- Exploration-play
	- Balanced success
	- Economic status
	- Intellectual orientation
- Possibly two broad classes of goals
	1. Individual striving
	2. Harmony seeking
- Alternate approach to goal dimensionality focuses on **categorizing life domains**^[similar to what I am doing!]
- Ford  and Nichols's heuristic two-part hierarchy

**FUTURE TAXONOMY**
