# Notes
## Summary
>[!Quote]
>When studied systematically, heterogeneity can be leveraged to 
build more complete theories of causal mechanism that could inform nuanced and dependable guidance to policymakers.

**The [[heterogeneity revolution]] will be defined by:**
1. presumption that [[intervention]] effects are [[context dependent]]
2. skepticism of insufficiently qualified claims about an intervention’s ‘true effect’ that ignore or downplay heterogeneity
3. understanding that variation in effect estimates across replications is to be expected even in the absence of [[type 1 error]]

**Focus on behavioral science and policy:**
>[!Quote]
>We  define  this  field  as  research  aimed  at  harnessing  basic
insights  from  psychology  and  behavioural  economics  to  develop  **interventions** that advance policy goals without using mandates or  significant  changes  in  economic  incentives.

Three main categories of [[intervention]]:
1. [[nudging]]: [[cognitive psychology]] and [[behavioral economics]]
2. [[wise interventions]]: [[social psychology]]
3. behavioural  intervention  work  conducted  in research communities that are focused on specific policy domains,  such  as  health,  education,  environmental  conservation  and  economic development.



## Thoughts
Seems like idea of [[dynamic regime]]s would make sense in this context...good for unknown heterogeneity as you fit the treatment to the subject over time...

Feel like their lists/categorizations are a bit sloppy...

---

My take away was that improving our methods and reducing type one error is important but will only get us so far if we just focus on main effects and ignore heterogeneity. 

We may not know what covariates matter, or what moderators exist in any given experiment but if we understand the phenomenon we are investigating we probably have some theoretical intuitions. And can just look at other data that is collected as well. 

Focus on demographic groups a bit narrow...this is more about individual differences and context/environment differences...(schools with norms supportive of growth mindsets)
- these could or could not be the differences that matters for a given treatment or question...

Idea of how we do science...seems a bit separate from the heterogeneity question...

other than the idea that understanding heterogeneity requires "larger" projects (reference to Duckworth and Behavioral Change for Good mega studies)
