# Notes
## Summary
### Chat GPT
>The study replicated the classic Marshmallow Test, in which children were offered a choice between an immediate small reward or a delayed larger reward. The researchers found that children who were able to delay gratification had better outcomes in adulthood, such as higher educational attainment and better emotional regulation. However, the relationship between early delay of gratification and later outcomes was weaker than in previous studies, suggesting that other factors, such as socioeconomic status, may also play a role in determining life outcomes. The study highlights the importance of considering multiple factors in understanding the complex relationship between early childhood experiences and later life outcomes.

### Human
Replication and extension of Shoda, Mischel, and Peake’s (1990) famous marshmallow study.

>[!Important]
>Concentrating on children whose mothers had not completed college

**RESULTS**
- found that an additional minute waited at age 4 predicted a gain of approximately one tenth of a standard deviation  in achievement at age 15
- bivariate correlation was only half the size of those reported in the original studies
- correlation was reduced  by  two  thirds  in  the  presence  of  controls  for  family  background,  early  cognitive  ability,  and  the   home  environment.
- Most  of  the  variation  in  adolescent  achievement  came  from  being  able  to  wait  at  least  20s.
- Associations  between  delay  time  and  measures  of  behavioral  outcomes  at  age  15  were  much  smaller  and  rarely   statistically significant.
- observed that delay of gratification was **strongly correlated with concurrent measures of cognitive ability**, and controlling for a composite measure of self-control explained  only about 25% of our reported effects on achievement.  These  results  suggest  that  the  marshmallow  test  may   capture  something  rather  distinct  from  self-control.^[self-control + intelligence is a bit like the grit measure...]
