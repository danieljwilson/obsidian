# Notes
A larger sample size implies lower measurement errors and more, stable factor loadings, replicable factors, and generalizable results to the true population structure ([59](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2018.00149/full#B59), [65](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2018.00149/full#B65))

==sample size determination for psychometric validation studies is rarely ever justified *a priori*==

> Approximately 92% of the articles reported a subject to item ratio greater than or equal to 2, whereas 25% had a ratio greater than or equal to 20. 
> 
> Data were extracted from 114 relevant articles. Within these, sample size determination was low (9.6%, 11/114), and were reported as either an arbitrary minimum sample size (n = 2), a subject to item ratio (n = 4), or the method was not explicitly stated (n = 5). Very few articles (4%, 5/114) compared _a posteriori_ their sample size to a subject to item ratio. 
> 
> (Anthoine et al., 2014)

>The necessary sample size is dependent on several aspects of any given study, including the level of variation between the variables, and the level of over-determination (i.e., the ratio of variables to number of factors) of the factors ([59](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2018.00149/full#B59)). The rule of thumb has been at least 10 participants for each scale item, i.e., an ideal ratio of respondents to items is 10:1 ([60](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2018.00149/full#B60)). However, others have suggested sample sizes that are independent of the number of survey items. Clark and Watson ([29](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2018.00149/full#B29)) propose using 300 respondents after initial pre-testing. Others have recommended a range of 200–300 as appropriate for factor analysis ([61](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2018.00149/full#B61), [62](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2018.00149/full#B62)). Based on their simulation study using different sample sizes, Guadagnoli and Velicer ([61](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2018.00149/full#B61)) suggested that a minimum of 300–450 is required to observe an acceptable comparability of patterns, and that replication is required if the sample size is < 300. Comrey and Lee suggest a graded scale of sample sizes for scale development: 100 = poor, 200 = fair, 300 = good, 500 = very good, ≥1,000 = excellent ([63](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2018.00149/full#B63)). Additionally, item reduction procedures (described, below in Step 5), such as parallel analysis which requires bootstrapping (estimating statistical parameters from sample by means of resampling with replacement) ([64](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2018.00149/full#B64)), may require larger data sets.
