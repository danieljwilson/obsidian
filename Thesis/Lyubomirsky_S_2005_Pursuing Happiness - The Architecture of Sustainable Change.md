# Notes
## Summary
Define happiness that they are interested in as "a  quantity  that  is  more  enduring  than  momentary or daily happiness but that is also somewhat malleable  over  time"...they term this ==chronic happiness level==...
>[!Important]
>the  authors  propose  that  a  person’s  chronic  happiness
level is governed by **3 major factors**:
1 | a genetically determined set point for happiness
2 | happiness-relevant  circumstantial  factors,
3 | happiness-relevant activities and practices

>[!QUote]
>happiness has numerous positive byproducts that appear to benefit   individuals,   families,   and   communities (Lyubomirsky, King, & Diener, 2004; see also Fredrickson, 2001)
>
>...happy individuals appear more likely  to  be  flourishing  people,  both  inwardly and outwardly

##### Can we change our levels of happiness/[[well being|well-being]]?
**Pessimism**
- Idea of a genetically determined "set point" that we will return to (Suh,  Diener,  & Fujita, 1996)
- Like personality traits (i.e. big 5), happiness is fairly consistent within subject (Costa, McCrae, & Zonderman, 1987; Diener & Lucas, 1999)
- idea of [[hedonic treadmill]]...any gains in happiness are temporary as we quickly adapt to the changes (Brickman & Campbell, 1971)
>[!Note]
>I think that part of the issue is just semantic...they don't really tightly define what they mean by happiness...and make comparisons to "ecstasy" which I see as more on the pleasure end of the spectrum. I am thinking more about well-being or flourishing or [[eudaimonia]]...

**Optimism**
- emerging research in the [[positive psychology]] tradition  demonstrating  that  practicing  certain virtues, such as gratitude (Emmons & McCullough, 2003), forgiveness (McCullough, Pargament, & Thoresen, 2000), and thoughtful self- reflection (King, 2001; Lyubomirsky, Sousa, & Dickerhoof,  2004),  can  bring  about  enhanced well-being
- many different motivational and attitudinal factors have been linked to well-being, factors  that  are  presumably  amenable  to  some volitional  control.

##### 3 Factor Model of [[chronic happiness]]
![[Pasted image 20220621095732.png]]
**SET POINT**
- Genetically  determined  and  is  assumed  to  be  fixed,  stable  over time, and immune to influence or control. 
- Consistent   with   this   assumption,   twin   studies (Lykken  &  Tellegen,  1996;  Tellegen  et  al., 1988),   long-term   panel   studies   (Headey   & Wearing, 1989), and studies of the effects of life events on well-being (Brickman et al., 1978) all indicate  substantial  long-term  stability  in  happiness.

**CIRCUMSTANCES**
- happiness-relevant circumstantial factors
- location, demographics, personal history, job, status, income, relationship, etc.
- all  circumstances combined account for only 8% to 15% of  the  variance  in  happiness  levels  (Argyle, 1999;  Diener  et  al.,  1999)
- once basic needs are met circumstances will generally just lead back to your set point due to hedonic adaptation

**INTENTIONAL ACTIVITY**
- discrete actions or practices in which  people  can  choose  to  engage, assuming  that  these intentional  activities  require some degree of effort to enact
- behavioral activity, such as  exercising  regularly  or  trying  to  be  kind  to others,  are  associated  with  well-being  (e.g., Keltner & Bonanno, 1997; Magen & Aharoni, 1991)
- also cognitive activity, such as reframing situations in a more positive light or pausing to count one’s blessings (Em- mons & McCullough, 2003; King, 2001; Seligman, 1991), and some kinds of volitional activity, such as striving for important personal goals (Sheldon  &  Houser-Marko,  2001)  or  devoting effort   to   meaningful   causes   (M.   Snyder   & Omoto, 2001)
- They do note that following through on these intentions can be difficult!

##### Implementation...
Need a ==person-activity fit==
- i.e. [[heterogeneous treatment]] based on [[individual differences]]
- certain  kinds  of  experiences  are  likely  to  be beneficial to anyone, because these experiences reflect universal psychological needs (e.g. experiences that involve belongingness (Baumeister & Leary, 1995), self-efficacy (Bandura,  1997),  or  autonomy  (Deci  &  Ryan, 2000))
