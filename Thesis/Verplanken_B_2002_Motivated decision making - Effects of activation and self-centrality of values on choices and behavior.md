## Chat GPT
The paper investigates how values influence decision-making and behavior. 

The authors suggest that the activation and self-centrality of values can influence the extent to which individuals act in line with their values. 

The paper presents several experiments that support this hypothesis, showing that values that are more central to individuals' self-concept are more likely to be activated in the decision-making process, and that activation of these values can lead to greater consistency between values and behavior. The authors also suggest that this effect can be influenced by various factors, such as the context in which decisions are made and the salience of competing values. 

Overall, the paper highlights the importance of understanding how values are activated and how they influence decision-making, and provides a useful framework for future research in this area.

## Summary
We have greater motivation to do things we value.

>[!quote]
>Values were thus found to give meaning  to,  energize,  and  regulate  value-congruent  behavior,  but  only  if  values  were  cognitively activated and central to the self.

>[!quote]
>Taken together, the studies show that values do not influence behavior by default. Rather, both activation  and  the  centrality  of  a  value  to  the  self  constitute necessary  elements  for  value-guided  behavior.

### Experiments
Experiment 1 investigated the effects of value activation on decision making. The participants were randomly assigned to one of two conditions: value-activation or control. In the value-activation condition, participants were asked to write about a personal experience that made them feel good about a particular value. In the control condition, participants were asked to write about a neutral topic. The results showed that participants in the value-activation condition were more likely to choose an option that aligned with their activated value.

Experiment 2 investigated the effects of self-centrality of values on decision making. The participants completed a survey to identify their most important values and then completed a decision-making task that required them to choose between two options that differed in terms of how they aligned with the participant's most important value. The results showed that participants were more likely to choose the option that aligned with their most important value, regardless of whether it was an intrinsic or extrinsic value.

Experiment 3 investigated the effects of self-centrality of values on behavior. The participants completed the same survey as in Experiment 2, but then engaged in a behavior that required effort and could be related to their most important value or not. The results showed that participants were more likely to engage in the effortful behavior when it was related to their most important value.

## Thoughts
Focus on ==Value -> Behavior== which I think is interesting but seems key to consider what the intentions/goals are. If behavior is not aligned with values is it because we are not enacting our intentions or just that we are setting poor goals?

I don't agree with their claim that living up to a value fulfills an abstract goal. I think the abstract goal IS trying to live up to or embody a value. i.e. I want to be a good father, or a moral person, or a courageous person...etc.

>Values are motivational constructs^[What exactly does this mean?
