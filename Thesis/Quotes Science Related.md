> Some chilling papers appeared too. The NARPS paper showed us how seventy teams of analysts looking at the same set of fMRI data agreed on almost nothing about which hypotheses those data supported. As I wrote [earlier this year](https://medium.com/the-spike/seventy-teams-of-scientists-analysed-the-same-brain-data-and-it-went-badly-e0d96c23dbf4), nothing about the failures in this paper were specific to fMRI research — the authors were showing us how any area of science with complex data processing and analysis pipelines could have teams of brilliant, smart people end up at polar opposite conclusions from the exact same data.

> And [a stuff-of-nightmares paper](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-020-2176-1) for any modeller showed that a widely-used model for estimating the dynamics of the evolution of existing species could produce an infinite number of evolutionary trees consistent with collected data. So any inferences made from that model are garbage. Cue a bonfire of papers, and computational people from all other disciplines muttering “there but for the grace of god go I” under their breath.

>a lot of people (me included) worry about shoddy inferences in psychology. and we talk a lot about all the things we could/should do to improve things. and that's all well and good. but the very fact that any of this needs to be said at all should arguably worry us far more. 
>you don't see engineers writing papers in 2021 saying "it's important to get basic math right, or any bridges we build will fall down." they don't have to do, because the first few times bridges fell down, people immediately moved to fix their math.
>psychologists have been using roughly the same methods for about a century. and for about that long, people have been complaining about abuses of p-values and confidence intervals and how terrible the consequences are for our science.
>the problem is, it's a lie. the consequences \*aren't\* terrible. if they were, nature would have corrected us by now. somewhere, somehow, the psychological equivalent of bridges would have fallen down. and we would have improved our practices as a result.
>the root problem isn't bad training. it isn't that psychologists aren't statsy or theoretical enough. it isn't misaligned incentives. it's that most of psychology makes no contact with the real world, so the real world can't apply any reciprocal pressure on psychology.
>the question we should ask ourselves isn't "why are people so bad at p-values?" people are bad at p-values because p-values are hard! no surprise there. BUT: calculus is also hard to understand, yet engineers learn calculus well enough that bridges don't fall down everywhere.
>the question we should ask ourselves is: "why are people \*still\* so bad at p-values?" making errors when you start out is expected—in every field. but going an entire career without reality ever correcting even basic misconceptions? how could that be normal?
>i think this should worry us. a lot. it's all well and good to say that we need better training, incentives, etc... but the real implication is that \*it might not even matter\*.
>if prolonged contact with empirical data can't be relied to correct even very basic misconceptions about our standard inferential tools, how could we trust the same methods to assess the quality of our ideas, let alone to effect positive changes on the world?
>postscript: i wasn't very clear on what i meant by "connects with the real world". i don't mean "has an impact on people's day-to-day lives", but rather "the empirical measurements are very close to the inferential target"
>- Tal Yarkoni [Twitter thread](https://twitter.com/talyarkoni/status/1350905491262738432)

[[Nathanial Daw]] (from Brain Inspired podcast episode 100.2, minute 17:00)
- Princeton
>I think the big challenge is how to deal with large amounts of data. Not just in the sense of statistics, but how to ask a question to which the answer is "record a 1000 neurons". Or how to form a hypothesis about how the brain works that you can answer by recording 1000 neurons. I think part of the challenge is that faced with all this data it is very easy to do descriptive things, and a lot of the fancy trends in neuroscience are just ways of describing large amounts of data - like manifolds and graph theory and networks. They are just ways of writing down facts of ensembles of measurements and simplifying them to some extent but they ddon't really answer questions. It is hard to even know what the question is for which the answer is "all this data".

MISTAKEN FUNDING PRIORITIES AT NIMH (from NYT article)
>Dr. Allen Frances, a professor emeritus of psychiatry at Duke University School of Medicine, warned [in 2014 that the institute](https://www.nytimes.com/2014/02/04/science/blazing-trails-in-brain-science.html) was “betting the house on the long shot that neuroscience will come up with answers to help people with serious mental illness.”

>In an interview last week, Dr. Frances, 79, said his warnings had been borne out.

>“The end result of these last 30 years is an exciting intellectual adventure, one of the more fascinating pieces of science in our lifetimes, but it hasn’t helped a single patient,” he said.

>He added that it was difficult, surveying the homeless camps proliferating in so many American cities, to feel proud of his 55 years of work in the field. People with severe mental illnesses, he said, were “much better off” then than now.

>“I have a happy life, and I’m not spending every minute of my life feeling guilty, but if I look back on my career, it’s with regret, not with satisfaction,” he said.
