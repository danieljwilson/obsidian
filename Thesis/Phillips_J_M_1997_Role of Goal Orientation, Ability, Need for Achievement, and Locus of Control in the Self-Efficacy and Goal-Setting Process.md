# Notes
## Summary
==Goal is to integrate ability, goal setting, self-efficacy and multiple personality traits into a common framework to explain and predict individual performance.==

They believe [[self-efficacy]] and [[goal level]] affect [[motivation]].

> [!QUOTE]
> Personality traits can influence the motivational process at various stages

> [!NOTE]
> **Setting harder goals -> better performance**
> (Hollenbeck  & Brief,  1987; Locke  &  Latham,  1990)

**What influences the goals we set?** One view is:
- self-perceptions of ability
- dispositional variables
	- e.g. need for achievement

>[!Quote]
>Goal-setting   studies  that  have   included   individual   difference   variables   have   typically treated  individual differences as **potential  moderators**,  ==not as  predictors  of  self-set  goal  level==  (Campbell,   1982).

**Proposed Theoretical Model**
![[Pasted image 20220607102426.png]]

##### Measures
1. Learning and performance goal orientations 
2. [[need for achievement]]
3. [[locus of control]]
4. [[ability]] (ACT and SAT scores)
5. Goals (had students report their exam goals)
6. [[self-efficacy]]
7. Performance (grade on next exam)
8. Indicator variables

##### Results
- Overall,  ability,  learning  and performance  goal  orientations,  and  locus  of  control  accounted  for  ==20%  of  the  variance  in  self-efficacy==
- Self- efficacy  and  need  for  achievement  accounted  for  ==33%  of the variance in goal level==
- Self-efficacy,  goal level, and ability  accounted for ==30% of the variance in performance==

>[!IMPORTANT]
>This is the first study known by the authors to identify self-efficacy  and  goal-setting  processes  as  mediators  of the  effect  of  the  personality  traits  of  locus  of  control  and learning  and  performance  goal  orientations  on  performance.


## Thoughts
Hm. Seems like another version of the [[theory of planned behavior]]? Where self-set goal = intention?

How do they confirm the structure of their model? Just seems like they removed arrows that weren't deemed significant...they do have some RMSEA scores and such comparing models but I am not sure that they compared all the models? What about just having everything as a variable predicting performance? 

They suggest interventions to increase self-efficacy in order to improve motivation and performance. But we don't see any arrows from self-efficacy to motivation - except through "self-set goal" which you can assume is associated with motivation but it is not the same thing and you would imagine the causal arrow leads from motivation to self-set goal and not the other way...

Also how malleable is self-efficacy to begin with?
