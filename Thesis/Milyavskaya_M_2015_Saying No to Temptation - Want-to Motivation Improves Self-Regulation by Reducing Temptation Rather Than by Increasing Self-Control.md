# Notes
## Summary
### Chat GPT
The first study used a survey to measure "want-to" motivation, self-control, and the occurrence of temptation in participants' daily lives. The results showed that "want-to" motivation was negatively associated with the occurrence of temptation, and this relationship was stronger than the positive relationship between self-control and the occurrence of temptation.

The second study used an experimental design to manipulate "want-to" motivation and measure its effect on self-regulation. Participants were randomly assigned to one of two groups: a "want-to" motivation group or a control group. The "want-to" motivation group was instructed to write about their personal goals and why they wanted to achieve them, while the control group was instructed to write about a neutral topic. The results showed that the "want-to" motivation group had fewer temptations and performed better on a self-regulation task than the control group.

The third study used a similar experimental design to the second study but also included a measure of self-control strength. The results showed that the "want-to" motivation manipulation reduced temptation and improved self-regulation, regardless of participants' self-control strength.

### Human
>[!Goal]
>In this article we investigate this question, looking at the role of **goal motivation** on the **impulsive** and **effortful** components of **self-regulation**.

Goal motivation can be "have-to" or "want-to"^[seems aligned with externally vs internally motivated goals...also the success rate in achieving each categories of goals differs between people I would imagine] (see [[self-determination theory]])

Define self-regulation/self-control as:
>the effortful inhibition of impulses or the overcoming of temptations

>[!quote]
>we use “self-regulation” as a broad term referring to all manner of goal pursuit, which can include both effortful control of behavior, but also effortless, automatic, or habitual forms of goal directed behavior (de Ridder, Lensvelt- Mulders, Finkernauer, Stok, & Baumeister, 2012; Fujita, 2011). As such, self-regulation often consists of balancing self-control efforts against the presence and strength of temptations and impulses that can distract or impede one’s goal pursuits and must be effortfully overcome.

**RESULTS** 
==Study 1==
People with greater want-to motivation for eating healthy had a more positive automatic response to healthy foods and a less positive one to unhealthy foods.

==Study 2==
This second study conceptually replicates the results of the first study, showing that want-to motivation is related to greater implicit preference for goal-promoting rather than goal-thwarting stimuli.^[Seems like we are getting at the fairly intuitive idea that internally motivated goals are easier to achieve than externally motivated goals (on average)]

>[!Note]
>The first two studies demonstrated that **want-to goals** are related to **lower automatic desire for potential temptations** that conflict with the goal. Additionally, Study 2 showed that these **lower automatic desires are translated into subjectively experiencing fewer obstacles** standing in the way of goal pursuit.

==Study 3==
- Test (again) whether want-to and have-to motivation influence people’s **subjective experiences of obstacles**
- Look at subsequent goal progress
- **Contrast** the effects of **motivation** on the **impulsive system** (as indexed by obstacles) with effects on **actual effort applied** in the pursuit of the goal, which would represent the reflective system
- Look beyond eating goals to see if our earlier studies would **generalize** to other domains

>[!Description]
> 1. participants described three goals they planned to pursue during the semester. 
> 2. Six weeks later, they reported on the obstacles they encountered and the effort they put into pursuing the goal. 
> 3. Finally, at the end of the semester, they reported on their goal progress. 
>    
>   We explored whether **want-to and have-to motivation** played a role in the **obstacles** that participants encountered and the amount of effort invested into pursuing each goal.

>[! Results]
>while want-to motivation was related to experiencing significantly fewer obstacles, have-to motivation was related to experiencing significantly greater obstacles.

## Thoughts
Not completely sure I understand the distinction that is being drawn. Seem to be pointing out that "impulse modulation" is frequently overlooked - but how is this distinct from effortful control of behavior?

I do agree that not all goal-directed behavior is effortful, and understand that strength and frequency of cravings is an important component of self-control and goal attainment but then they go back to talk about goal motivation, which seems like yet another separate component.  

How motivated you are for the goal that REQUIRES self-regulation seems important.
How strong the temptations/desires/impulses that you experience that are unaligned with your goal seems important.

Seems like impulses are always seen as negative? What if you impulsively do something aligned with your goals? e.g. sweep the floor, or go online to buy an item you need, or read about a topic of interest?

In talking about goal conflict it seems like the insinuation is that the conflicting "temptations" are bad, and unaligned with ANY goals you have. It could just be that they are unaligned with that specific goal but aligned with other goals...

>Recent research has used this methodology to study desires, and has found that people experience many desires each day, 35% of which conflict with important goals, **constituting a temptation**
