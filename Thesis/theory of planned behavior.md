# Notes
States that behavioral achievement depends on both [[motivation]] (intention) and ability (behavioral control)

Specifies three determinants of intention to perform a given behavior (only the first two are in the [[Theory of Reasoned Action]])^[original model had limitations in terms of dealing with behaviors over which people have incomplete volitional control]:
1. **attitude toward the behavior**: the person’s overall evaluation of the performing the behavior
2. **subjective norm**: the person’s perceived expectations of important others with regard to his or her performing the behavior in question
3. **perceived behavioral control**: the extent to which the individual feels he or she has control over performing the behavior, or the perceived ease of performing the behavior.


**Example**
>people will have strong intentions to perform a given action if they evaluate it positively, believe that important others would want them to perform it, and think that it is easy to perform.

- distinguishes between three types of beliefs - behavioral, normative, and control
- comprised of six constructs that collectively represent a person's actual control over the behavior
	- This is not clear in the diagram? ==Actual Control has no arrows going into it!==
- started as the [[Theory of Reasoned Action]] in 1980^[Ajzen & Fishbein, 1980; Fishbein & Ajzen, 1975]
- key component is [[behavioral intent]]
- has been used successfully to predict and explain a wide range of health behaviors and intentions including smoking, drinking, health services utilization, breastfeeding, and substance use
