# Notes
## Summary
### Chat GPT
The study found that individuals who possess high levels of self-control and resist temptation are more successful in achieving their goals and avoiding depletion than those who lack self-control. 

Moreover, effortful self-control, rather than automatic self-control, was found to be a stronger predictor of goal attainment and depletion. 

The study concludes that self-control is an important factor in predicting success in real-life settings and highlights the significance of developing and maintaining self-control to achieve long-term goals.

### Human
**IDEA**
Self-control is typically viewed as a key ingredient responsible for effective self-regulation and personal goal attainment.

>[!Definition]
>Known colloquially as willpower, **effortful self-control** is the ability to restrain one’s impulses^[wouldn't it be accurate to say that temptation bundling is effectively **leveraging** your impulses?] in the service of greater goals and priorities.

**METHODS**
The study used experience sampling, daily diary, and prospective data collection to investigate the immediate and semester-long consequences of effortful self-control and temptations on depletion and goal attainment.

**RESULTS**
- goal attainment was influenced by experiences of temptations rather than by actively resisting or controlling those temptations^[this would seem like a vote in favor of creating systems/structures that minimize temptation exposure, or by leveraging those temptations in productive ways...]
- simply experiencing temptations led people to feel depleted
- Depletion in turn mediated the link between temptations and goal attainment, such that people who experienced increased temptations felt more depleted and thus less likely to achieve their goals. 
- people who exerted more overall self-control did **not** actually report more overall depletion
- Critically, results of Bayesian analyses ==strongly indicate that effortful self-control was consistently unrelated to goal attainment throughout all analyses==^[this seems very counter intuitive...].

>[!Conclusion]
>Our results suggest that the path to better self-regulation lies not in increasing self-control but in removing the temptations available in our environments.
