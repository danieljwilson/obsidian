Talks about paper ==Judgment Under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases==
>[!Key Point]
>While heuristics can be useful, they can also lead to systematic biases or errors in judgment.

Paper talks about a few main types of heuristics:

1.  **Representativeness Heuristic**: This refers to the tendency of people to judge the probability of an event by considering how similar it is to the prototypes of the event. This can often lead to neglect of relevant statistical properties, such as base rates.
2.  **Availability Heuristic**: This is the process of judging the frequency or likelihood of an event based on how easily instances or occurrences can be brought to mind. This can often lead to errors, such as overestimating the likelihood of memorable but rare events.
3.  **Anchoring and Adjustment Heuristic**: This heuristic involves making decisions based on an initial value (the "anchor") and then adjusting away from that anchor. People often under-adjust, leading to anchoring bias.
