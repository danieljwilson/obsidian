# Notes
## Summary
>[!Quote] quantitative review of the link between successful goal pursuit and subjective well-being

>[!Quote] we assume that goal progress and SWB are positively related and that this relationship is generalizable

>Our  meta-analysis  of  the  overall  effect  of  successful  goal  pursuit  yielded  a  medium-to- large average effect size (q = .43).

>successful goal striving is one of the most important correlates of SWB, followed by health (q = .32, Okun et al. 1984), socioeco-nomic status (q = .20, Howell and Howell 2008), and personality (q = .19, DeNeve and Cooper 1998).2

>Our  results  show,  that  the  relationship  between  successful  goal  striving  and  SWB  is stronger if goals are assessed ideographically and successful pursuit is measured as progress  compared  to  an  assessment  via  goal  lists  and  attainment  measures,  respectively.^[contradicts Carver
and  Scheier  (1998)  who  suppose  that  goal  attainment  is  a  dead  end]

>We  argue  that  these  findings  can  be  best  interpreted  by  following  a  strictly  hierarchical understanding of goals as suggested by the goal system theory by Kruglanski et al. (2002). If the goal is well integrated into a hierarchical network of goals, the attainment offers a new sense of direction (towards the next higher-order goal) and therefore has the potential  to  enhance  SWB.  If,  however,  the  goal  is  not  well  integrated,  the  dead  end metaphor might be appropriate.


- 108 independent  samples  derived  from  85  studies
- significant  association between successful goal striving and SWB (p = .43)
- Moderator analyses showed that the association was larger when:
	1. successful goal pursuit was defined as goal progress, instead of goal attainment
	2. SWB was measured as SWB (positive indicators), instead of ill-being (negative indicators)
	3. the SWB measure matched the goal content, instead of  lacked  conceptual  correspondence
	4. the  data  collection  took  place  in  an individualistic culture, instead of a collectivistic culture

Empirical findings support the view that striving for personal goals is linked to SWB (e.g., Brunstein 1993; Cantor and Sanderson 2003; Emmons 1986).

**COUNTERPOINT**
considering the image of a ‘‘hedonic treadmill’’ by Brickman and Campbell (1971) which illustrates the assumption that any change in  the  individual’s  SWB  level  will  only  be  temporary,  a  sustained  enhancement  seems doubtful...

Their focus is on **successful goal pursuit**, as =="most proximal predictor"== of [[subjective well-being]]

##### Subjective Well Being
**Hedonism Approach**
- equals SWB with happiness in respect of pleasure attainment and  pain  avoidance
**Eudaemonic Approach**
- reflects  the  view  that  SWB consists  of  the  actualization  of  human  potentials  (Ryan  and  Deci 2001)

**Possible to Improve?**
- Lykken  and  Tellegen  ( 1996)  suggest  that  people  have  a  biologically  determined happiness set point which is unchangeable.
- second process that might prevent sustainable  SWB  is [[hedonic treadmill]]  (Kahnemann 2003)

**SWB construct comprises both emotional and cognitive components.**
- emotional component consists of positive and negative affect while the cognitive aspect represents the evaluative judgment of life satisfaction

##### Goal Interaction
>[!Quote]
>it is not clear how the goal progress evaluation of one goal is set off against  the  evaluations  of  other  goals.  Goals  are  not  detached  entities,  but  rather anchored within a goal network. This is inevitable to meet the challenges of the increasingly complex world. Consequently, individuals strive for more than one goal at the same time and these goals might facilitate or hinder the realization of each other (Riediger and Freund 2004). Unfortunately, primary studies included in our analysis do not address the question of potential  additive or interactive effects  of simultaneously goal pursuit. Even though  some  of  them  assessed  more  than  one  goal,  they  reported  neither  an  index  of hindrance or facilitation nor any goal characteristic separately for the different goals. What needs  to  be  investigated  further  is  how  individuals  manage  divergent  perceptions  of multiple goals characteristics at the same time.
##### Possible Problem
>[!Quote]
>there might be a conceptual overlap between goals and SWB that makes it difficult to interpret the findings and draw generalizable conclusions. As Wiese (2007) stated, personal goals are often formulated as well-being goals (e.g., to reach and maintain martial happiness; to be satisfied with one’s life). If this is the case, it is really difficult if not impossible to distinguish  between  goal  progress  and  SWB  as  an  outcome  criteria.
