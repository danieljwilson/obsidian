# Notes
## Summary
Idea behind [[dual-process model]] is that [[intuition]] and [[deliberation]] are two **qualitatively** different processes.

[[single-process model]] proponents claim that the differences are a matter of degree and not kind.

[[Wim De Neys]] claim is that ==there is currently no good evidence that allows us to decide the debate==.

---

### Key points of the arguments
**Feature alignment**
 perfect, natural alignment can be used to argue for the existence of qualitative differences. The fact that intuition and deliberation are so perfectly contrasted on so many dimensions, might give the impression that they must be two fundamentally different mental “species”.
 
 However...

- non-alignment does not argue against the dual process position
-  alignment question is orthogonal to the dual vs single process discussion
-  incubation phenomenon as an example of non-alignment
	-  idea that a difficult question will get solved in the background and just "come to you"
-  BUT not necessary for there to be qualitative difference...as long as there exists a qualitative difference along ==one single dimension==, there is no need for such a difference along multiple features

**Defining features**
In contrast to perfect feature alignment this is the idea that there is only a requirement of a single critical "defining feature" to decide whether we are dealing with an intuitive or deliberative process. Everything else (the other features) are just correlated.

However...

- determining threshold for continuous features problematic

==Working memory==
- different definition of working memory...theoretical vagueness
- means that hard to draw sharp distinction between processes that require [[working memory]] and those that do not.
- claims that working memory is equivalent to [[attentional control]]^[I think that I have an issue with this. Seems to be moving the goalposts in the sense that he shows instances in which attentional control is unconsciously enacted and then makes claims about working memory based on this...]

==Cognitive decoupling and mental simulation==
e.g. hypothetical thinking^[Again his argument against this feature seems unconvincing. Makes claims that things are not "cognitively demanding but doesn't specify his own threshold for this determination"]

==Autonomy==
Autonomous processes are conceived as those processes whose execution is mandatory when their triggering stimuli are encountered.
- Evans and Stanovich characterized autonomous processes as the opposite of processes that require mental simulation.
- Thompson (2013) argued, one can define intuitive processes as being autonomous and deliberate processes as non-autonomous processes without making any further assumptions about the nature of the non-autonomous processing (i.e., whether it involves a mental simulation process or not).^[agree with this]
- claims "there are no universally autonomous processes"^[what about algorithms/robots?]
- Evans updated definition: in addition to being autonomous, we are typically also conscious of the output of prototypical intuitive reasoning processes.

**Criterion S**
Criterion S is simultaneously held contradictory beliefs
-  Sloman reasoned that any qualitatively homogeneous reasoning system can provide but a single response at one time (i.e. two conflicting responses must be generaated by two qualitatively different reasoning systems)

**Parsimony**
Idea that if we can't distinguish between the two models that the simpler one should be selected...author argues that it isn't clear that the single process model is simpler/has fewer parameters

**Misinformation issue**
Weird point about how the field and general public might be misled?
>  the unsupported dichotomization will distort scientists’ conceptualizations and misinform the general public.


## Thoughts
- weird to call doing a math a "decision process'
- why can't there be two distinct systems based on evolutionary history that have mixed outputs. i.e. it is not all one or the other that controls our behavior.
- in that case it does make sense that we just can't distinguish between a continuous system and one that is a linear combination of two discrete systems.
