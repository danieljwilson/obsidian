### Existing Social Psychological Models

**[[Theory of Reasoned Action]]** (Fishbein 1980; Fishbein and Ajzen 1977)^[Seems like this later turned into the [[theory of planned behavior]]]
- intentions as proximal indicators of behavior
- predictors = ==attitudes== and ==subjective norms==

**[[Theory of Planned Behavior]]** (Ajzen 1991; Ajzen 1985)
- added in [[perceived behavioral control]]...
- Note that the theory of planned behavior **cannot distinguish** between inclined actors and inclined abstainers based on one study that was analyzed. 
	- From the study:
	  
	- The author states "I suspect  that  most  applications of  the  theory  of  planned  behavior  would  reveal  a  sizable  number  of  participants  with  equivalent  intentions,  perceived  behavioral  control, etc.,  who  nonetheless  differ  in  their  subsequent behavior." 

**[[Theory of Interpersonal Behavior|Attitude Behavior Theory]]** (Triandis 1980)
Also known as [[Theory of Interpersonal Behavior]]
- points out some behaviors closely tied to habit
- idea that "low facilitating conditions" can prevent performance of an intended behavior

**[[protection motivation theory|Protection Motivation Theory]]** (Maddux and Rogers 1983)
- predictors = ==threat appraisal== and ==coping appraisal==
- predictors mediated by behavioral intentions

What they have in common is that they all converge on the idea that the best predictor of behavior is a person's intention. Intention drives action. 
==Only...not always...==

### Properties of Behavioral Intentions
#### Temporal Stability
[[Theory of Reasoned Action]], [[theory of planned behavior]] both acknowledge that intentions can **change** prior to the performance of a behavior. We can change our minds! And sometimes with good reason.^[Seems tricky to assess the root reason for the change as we are so good at rationalizing...]
- Stability refers to the idea that as time passes intentions remain constant...the higher the stability the better predictor of behavior is an intention^[duh]

#### Degree of Intention Formation
Refers to the extent to which participants have thought through the consequences of their decision to act in a particular way.

>evidence suggests that well-formed intentions exhibit greater temporal stability than poorly formed intentions (Bagozzi & Yi, 1989).^[Again, duh.]

#### Attitudinal vs. Normative Control
Refers to how well cognitions about a behavior are related to intentions to perform that behavior. More succinctly: is the locus of control internal or external?
- in this case focus is on whether an intention is mainly driven by attitude (internally generated) or subjective norms (externally motivated)^[Seems like in many cases very hard to really separate these? Kind of the fish in water problem...]
- authors posit that [[self-determination theory]] suggests attitudinally driven intentions (with an internal locus of causality) should be more likely to be performed.

#### Certainty and Accessibility^[Seem like two different things?]
Certainty: ratings of how certain people are about their intention
Accessibility: measured by response latencies to intention items^[doesn't explain what it is]

- Find greater certainty/confidence in intention associated with better intention-behavior consistency
- Conflicting evidence on effects of accessibility on intention-behavior relationship

### Personality and Cognitive Variables
#### Action Control
- Kuhl (1985) has an [[Action-Control scale]]
- Can have **action** or **state** orientation
- Action orientation person focuses on actions required to reduce the discrepancy between their current and intended future state
- State orientation person focuses only on current or intended state^[Don't really get this? What would that look like in each scenario? What if you didn't like your current state and were focused on it?]

#### Anticipated Regret
- [[anticipated regret]] predictive of intention-behavior gap

#### Self-Schemas
- Self-schemas [[self-schema]] refer to people’s self-definitions in domains of enduring investment and concern
- Seems to be basically saying that we have smaller gaps if the action is aligned with our self-identity^[seems meaningful if you are trying to predict a gap in a specific domain]

#### Conflicting Intentions
- intentions that are negatively correlated, one with the other. For example having a great nightlife and also getting tenure. 
- the performance of one is likely to impede the other...
- A bid different than **competing intentions**, where the idea is that there can be two behaviors that are both "intentions" but they can't be done at the same time...e.g. study really hard for a test and also get 8 hours of sleep, perhaps.
- higher conflict -> bigger gap

### Questions
- How do all of these variables relate to each other?
- Does being schematic in the behavioral domain or possessing greater action orientation cause people to spontaneously form implementation intentions?
- Do more stable or well-formed intentions mean that participants can identify behaviors that are effective in achieving particular goals or mean that participants can shield their current intention from conflicting intentions?
- Which of the properties of behavioral intentions identified here is best capable of turning inclined abstainers into inclined actors?

### Conclusions
- From [[theory of planned behavior]]: Measure of past behavior often predicts future behavior better than a measure of intention.^[makes me think of revealed preference]
