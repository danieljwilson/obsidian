# Notes
## Summary
Quality of decisions heavily depends on the options we can come up with.

**Observation**
> Real-world decisions are often open ended, with goals, choice options, or evaluation criteria conceived by decision-makers themselves. Critically, ==the quality of decisions may heavily rely on the generation of options==, as failure to generate promising options limits, or even eliminates, the opportunity for choosing them.

**Gap**
Life is not a multiple choice test, yet this fact largely ignored by classical models of decision making.
- Many choices are **open-ended** or **[[ill-structured decision]]**

**What they claim to do**
>we take a step toward addressing this issue by developing a neurally inspired^[curious to see what they mean by this...i.e. how "directly" inspired] cognitive model of a class of ill-structured decisions in which choice options must be self-generated.

**Proposed Model**
- Includes `Semantic knowledge retrieval` + `Valuation processes`
- the ability to access memories can limit the options you can come up with
- dubbed a [[retrieval-constrained valuation model]]

**Model Stages**
1. Retrieval of semantic knowledge is a probabilistic process governed by the associative principle
2. Given a set of options, choice is governed by subjective preferences over the options, commonly referred to as utility or value

Predicts two reasons for not choosing an option:^[what about noise??]
1. It was not preferred
2. It was not recalled

**Experiment Design**
> We use behavioral responses from two distinct tasks capturing **valuation** and **semantic retrieval processes**, respectively, to ==predict behavior in a third task== that is hypothesized to require both processes

- between-subject design
- $N = 2811$
- Make choices in ==external/internal menu conditions==
- 6 categories (fruit, **fast food chains**, **running shoe brands**, vegetables, fish for dinner, salad dressings) where **bolded** were incentivized

**Hypothesis**
Constrained retrieval account predicts that highly (less) accessible items should benefit (suffer) under IMC due to the inability to retrieve less accessible options, irrespective of preference.^[This seems like a pretty safe and intuitive hypothesis. Easy to forget about something you actually like but encounter infrequently...]



## Thoughts
Seems like there is research/models that take into account option generation - but tend not to be specific about what those options might actually **be**...

- Had memory and option generation really not been linked before?
- Feels linked to time pressure - people don't want to spend much time on these tasks...not going to do an exhaustive memory search. Satisficing for good enough option...
- They claim that they "substantially expanding the explanatory and predictive scope of standard models of decision-making"...how so?
- What is the real world application? For any choice that matters I feel like I move to EMC via google/consulting others. What is an example of a situation where this would have life changing negative outcomes?
