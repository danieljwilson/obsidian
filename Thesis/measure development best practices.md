# Notes
**A good measurement requires:**^[From Fried & Flake, 2018]
1. Robust [[theory]] supporting the items you include in your scale
2. The scale has acceptable [[psychometric theory|psychometric properties]] (e.g., [[measure reliability]] and dimensionality^[Not sure what they mean by dimensionality])
3. The scale relates to other constructs in the ways hypothesized (e.g., [[convergent validity]] and [[discriminant validity]])
4. The scale captures group differences or causal processes expected to exist

**When using scales and measures:**
1. Clearly communicate the construct you aim to measure, how you define the construct, how you measure it, and the source of the measure.
2. Provide a rationale when using a specific scale over others or when modifying a scale. If possible, use multiple measures to demonstrate either robust evidence for a finding or the sensitivity of a finding to particular scales.
3. Stop using Cronbach’s alpha as a sole source of validity evidence. Alpha’s considerable limitations have been acknowledged and clearly described many times (e.g., Sijtsma, 2009). Alpha cannot stand alone in describing a scale’s validity.
