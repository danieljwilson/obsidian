# Notes
## Summary
>When individuals' represented actual self fulfills their goals about who they would ideally like to be or believe they ought to be, they feel good. When it does not, they feel bad (e.g., Cooley, 1902/ 1964; James, 1890/1948; Rogers, 1961; Stein & Jewett, 1982; for a review, see Higgins, 1987).

Goal is to find relation between emotional response and goal attainment. 
- What is a/the moderator?
- One proposed moderator = attitude strength (as attitude accessibility (Fazio 1986, 1995))
	- Higher attitude accessibility = higher correlation between attitude and behavior ^[Are we really looking for a correlation between attitude and behavior? Wouldn't attitude itself be below intention in the hierarchy? I might think of this as part of the "values" node in my model.]

Builds off work of Fazio (1986, 1995) and Bassili (1995, 1996) that operationalize [[attitude accessibility]] through [[response time]].

The [[regulatory focus strength measure]] was used to asses a **self-discrepancy measure** for both the actual-ideal and actual-ought gaps, as well as a **self-guide strength measure** based on response time^[The latter had no significant correlation between latency and magnitude of self-discrepancy.].

**Self guide strength** measure was based off of [[attitude accessibility]] concept...authors believe self-guides function as chronic goals, and self-guide strength can therefore be operationalized as self-guide accessibility (hence using the response time measurements).

- strong correlation between ought-actual and ideal-actual strength scores of $r = .73, p<.001$. 

They did 4 versions of the experiment (just skimmed)...notice a lot of $p<.05$s in the results...

**Conclusion**
>All four studies found that stronger regulatory focus was related to stronger emotional responses to goal attainment, with stronger promotion focus relating to stronger cheerfulness-dejection-related emotions and stronger prevention focus relating to stronger quiescence-agitation-related emotions.

Or, perhaps more simply, "chronic goal accessibility or strength can moderate emotional responses to chronic goal attainment."^[this seems logical...idea that if a goal is top of mind your self-discrepancy with that goal will be more personally salient...]

They believe that self-guide strength directly reflects the strength of regulatory focus, whereas the magnitude of self-discrepancy does not^[seems reasonable on its face...not impossible to have both high regulatory focus and high self-discrepancy. Many ways in which you could plausibly imagine that happening...Super high expectations of self...overly critical assessment of "actual" self, etc].
