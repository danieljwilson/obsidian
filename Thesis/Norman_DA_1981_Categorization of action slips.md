# Notes
## GPT4 Summary
>Donald Norman's categorization of action slips comes from his work on cognitive psychology, particularly in his book "The Design of Everyday Things." In this context, action slips refer to =unintentional actions that result from a lack of attention or automatic processing of routine tasks=. Norman distinguishes several different types of action slips, which help to understand how people interact with objects in their environment and why mistakes are often made.

>Here's a summary of the categorizations:

>1. **Capture Errors**: These occur when a more frequent or recent activity takes control over the intended action. An example would be intending to drive to the grocery store but finding yourself on the route to work because that's what you do every weekday morning.
    
>2. **Description Errors**: Description errors happen when the intended action has much in common with others, and you perform an action that is similar but wrong. For instance, grabbing a container of salt when you meant to grab sugar because they are kept in similar containers next to each other.
    
>3. **Data-Driven Errors**: These are mistakes that occur when external stimuli hijack your intended actions. For instance, typing the word that is spoken nearby instead of the word you intended to type.
    
>4. **Associative Activation Errors**: This type of slip happens when something in the environment activates an association that leads to the wrong action. For example, you might see a picture of a beach and start daydreaming about a vacation, leading you to miss your exit on the highway.
    
>5. **Loss-of-Activation Errors**: These occur when the intention is forgotten in the midst of performing it, perhaps due to interruption or distraction. An example would be going into a room to get something but forgetting what you went in there for once you arrive.
    
>6. **Mode Errors**: Mode errors occur when a device has different states or "modes" and the user forgets to switch between them, leading to unexpected outcomes. For example, using a digital device that remains in "caps lock" mode, leading to an unintended string of capitalized letters.

Donald Norman uses these categories to illustrate how cognitive processes operate during everyday activities and how they can go awry. Understanding these slips has implications for the design of more user-friendly objects and systems, as it emphasizes the need to align the design more closely with natural cognitive processes.

## Human Summary
>[!Quote]
>The  path from  ==intention to action==  consists of the  activation of the parent  schema that  corresponds  to  the  intention, the  activation  of  child  schemas  for  the  component parts of the  action sequence, and  then the appropriate triggering of schemas  when the  conditions match  those  required  for  their  operations.

Slips are **not intended** in the moment.

Focus of article is slips of **action**, rather than **words**

**Proposed model** = [[Activation-Trigger-Schema System]]
- assumes   that action  sequences  are  controlled  by  sensorimotor   knowledge  structures:  ==schemas== 
- this schema different than [[self-schema]]...idea is that a schema  is an  organized  memory unit, much along  the  lines proposed  for  perception  and memory
- requires  that   appropriate conditions  be  satisfied  for  the  operation  of a  schema (trigger)

>the   theory  allows  for  ==multiple sources  of activations==,  for  example  - from  the **external  world** (data-driven  activation),  
>- from **internal  processing**  (thoughts,   associations, prior  or  future  action  components),  or  
>- by capture by well-learned familiar **habits**.

**Slips  occur   for only  three  reasons:**
1. the  formation of  the  intention  is in  error;  
2. there  is faulty  activation of  schemas;  
3. there  is  a  failure  in  satisfying the  conditions for  triggering.

## Thoughts
- Doing actions vs NOT doing actions...

This is not really the form of intention-behavior gap I am interested in. Slips seem like something worth trying to minimize but it is not the same as deciding to skip a workout. 

These feel mostly minor - though why not try and avoid with intelligent design. Good use case for [[nudging]] perhaps. E.g. different visual cues for salt and pepper...

Intention behavior gap is usually a conflict with your 1st order and 2nd order preferences...you do actually INTEND to make the mistake IN THE MOMENT. For example, you decide to have a cigarette while out with friends even though you KNOW you are trying to quit.
