The criticisms regarding circularity and flaws in psychological measures are strong in some contexts and have been articulated in various ways. Here's a breakdown of some of the strongest arguments:

1. **Tautological Definitions**: Some critics argue that the way psychological constructs are defined and measured can be tautological. If a construct like intelligence is defined by the ability to perform well on an intelligence test, then using performance on the test as evidence for intelligence is circular.
    
2. **Lack of Objective Standards**: In physical sciences, measurements often refer to tangible and externally observable phenomena. In contrast, psychological constructs like self-esteem, anxiety, or creativity are abstract, and it can be argued that there are no objective standards against which to validate measures of these constructs.
    
3. **Reification**: Some critics argue that psychological constructs are often treated as if they were concrete, tangible things, rather than theoretical constructs. This can lead to a confusion between the construct and the measurement, creating a circular relationship.
    
4. **Reliance on Correlational Evidence**: To validate a measure, researchers often rely on correlations with other measures. If these other measures are also based on circular reasoning or weak theoretical foundations, then the validation can be flawed.
    
5. **Measurement Invariance**: Some measures might not be equivalent across different groups or cultures, leading to biased outcomes. If a measure is grounded in a particular cultural context, applying it to another culture without proper adaptation can lead to flawed interpretations.
    
6. **Reductionism**: The attempt to reduce complex psychological phenomena to numerical scores can be seen as overly simplistic and may miss essential aspects of the constructs being measured. This reductionist approach might not capture the full complexity of human behavior and experience.
    
7. **Over-Reliance on Quantitative Methods**: Some critics argue that the dominance of quantitative methods and standardized testing limits our understanding of human psychology by ignoring context, individual differences, and the nuanced and dynamic nature of psychological phenomena.
    
8. **Commercial Interests**: Some argue that the development of psychological tests is sometimes driven by commercial interests rather than scientific validity, leading to tests that are marketed aggressively despite lacking robust validation.
    
9. **Influence of Historical and Societal Biases**: Constructs like intelligence have been criticized for reflecting societal biases. The history of intelligence testing, for instance, includes episodes where tests were used to justify discrimination.
    
10. **Failure to Predict Real-World Behavior**: If a test claims to measure a construct like leadership ability but fails to predict leadership success in real-world settings, critics might argue that the measure is not only circular but also lacks utility.

These criticisms don't necessarily mean that all psychological measures are flawed or that they lack value. However, they do highlight the complexities and challenges in developing, interpreting, and applying psychological measures. The field continues to evolve, and many researchers are working to address these challenges, but the concerns remain an essential part of the debate in psychological measurement.
