## GPT4 Summary
The Theory of Reasoned Action (TRA) is a well-known model for understanding human behavior, originally developed by Martin Fishbein and Icek Ajzen. Blair Sheppard's work, "The Theory of Reasoned Action: A Meta-Analysis of Past Research with Recommendations for Modifications and Future Research," provides an in-depth examination of the theory, summarizing previous studies and suggesting areas for improvement.

Here's a summary of the key points:

1. **Basic Overview of TRA**: The Theory of Reasoned Action posits that a person's intention to perform a behavior is the most immediate determinant of that behavior. Intentions are shaped by attitudes toward the behavior (personal evaluation) and subjective norms (perceived social pressures).
    
2. **Meta-Analysis**: Sheppard performs a meta-analysis, aggregating the results of numerous studies that have utilized the TRA. This approach allows for a comprehensive view of how well the theory has performed across different contexts and populations.
    
3. **Findings**: The meta-analysis generally supports the TRA, demonstrating its effectiveness in predicting intentions and behaviors across various domains. However, the strength of the relationships between attitudes, subjective norms, intention, and behavior varied across different studies.
    
4. **Recommendations for Modifications**: Based on the analysis, Sheppard offers modifications to enhance the theory. This includes a recommendation to consider additional variables that might influence intention and behavior, such as moral norms, self-identity, and emotional factors.
    
5. **Limitations and Criticisms**: Sheppard acknowledges that the TRA might not apply equally well to all behaviors and all populations. The theory's simplicity, while a strength in many contexts, might overlook complex influences on behavior in certain situations.
    
6. **Future Research Directions**: The paper concludes with suggestions for future research, emphasizing the need for more longitudinal studies, exploring the dynamics of how intentions and behaviors change over time, and integrating the TRA with other theoretical frameworks to create a more comprehensive model.
    
7. **Implications for Practice**: The analysis and recommendations offer valuable insights for practitioners interested in behavior change, such as marketers, health promoters, and policy-makers, allowing them to create more effective interventions.

## Thoughts
Underlying focus on person as a "consumer"...
So to some degree not just trying to predict translation of intention to behavior but also trying to predict intentions...I am less interested in that

Goal vs. behavior intentions remind me of the hierarchy illustrated in the [[Carver_CS_1982_Control Theory - A Useful Conceptual Framework for Personality-Social, Clinical and Health Psychology|control theory paper by Carver]]. Also in the example of taking a diet pill vs. losing 10 pounds their could also be super and sub-ordinate goals.

>[!Quote]
>Actions  or outcomes were classified as goals if, in our  conservative opinion, there were major obstacles to the individuals' performance of the action or attainment of the goal.
>
>Certain actions occasionally  were classified as a goal for some samples of  subjects but as a behavior for others. For ex-  ample,  having sex or drinking an alcoholic  beverage would be considered goals for high  school  boys  because high  school  boys  may  lack the opportunity to perform such actions.

☝️ I think this is muddying the distinction between goals and behaviors. Isn't the goal to "be cool" and the behaviors to have sex and drink alcohol? Don't think it makes sense that they are turned into goals if difficult. There can be sub-behaviors that enable the behavioral intention of having sex of course...

I think the distinction of whether a situation **involves a choice** among alternatives is also flawed. Or maybe just too narrowly focused on economics/consumption.

They state that purchasing one model of a car over another is a "choice" but that they don't count the choice to buy a car in the first place vs. other transit modalities as a choice.
