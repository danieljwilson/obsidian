I was initially hesitant about writing this. I worried that it could come across as an attempt at persuasion. But that's not the aim - or at least I don't think that is the aim. I'm probably imperfect at recognizing my own ulterior motives.

To get straight to it, what follows below are some thoughts I wrote down about "kids" (i.e. having them). Prior to the arrival of Rilka and Amos I called up a number of friends that had recently had their own kids to get their thoughts and tips about the experience. It was a really interesting process, but one that reinforced how little attention I had paid to "kids" in general prior to having a couple on schedule to arrive. 

I was thinking about this the other day for some reason (the walks to and from daycare are fairly long) and the idea occurred to me that even if no one was currently asking **me** what it is like to have kids, I was interested in writing something down about the experience for my own future reference. Then I wondered whether this might be an interesting thing to share - a glimpse into one person's new parent experience (who you happen to share quite a bit of DNA with). This might not be interesting to anyone beyond my future self, and I am not offended at all if you don't make your way through it, or just file it away for later. But that's the back story.

So here is a brief synopsis on my first 18 months with kids (broken into a few categories).

**18 Months**
It's fairly common for couples to announce "we're pregnant", after getting that first positive test result and waiting the recommended 12 weeks. But they're not pregnant. The woman is pregnant. The man is just a prospective father. And you fairly often even, as the father, completely forget that fact. Then maybe you see a stroller or your partner and suddenly you remember. I think that this type of forgetting is much less common for the woman. It honestly does not seem very fair. 

The pregnancy time period was actually quite fun, on my end. Naturally, the physical experience complicates the fun for one party. But I think, overall, we both quite enjoyed it. Part of it is just that the whole concept is so bonkers. Somehow you are creating a new human that is kind of like you but also different than you and it is starting from a single cell? It's mind blowing in a college stoner "whoaaaa" way, but also just mind blowing, period. 

You have enough advance notice that there is time to really get into the planning and anticipation. We read numerous books, took notes, and watched videos each week about which fruit size the kids were and what new human attribute they were starting to develop. There were a few nervous moments during that time - Donna had some blood clotting and a DNA test revealed that she also had the pre-mutation for fragile-x which can be pretty devastating for boys - but I think that overall things went pretty smoothly. Of course none of the preparation we did really "prepared" us for the experience of having kids. But I think that we knew that would be the case. 

The birth process itself feels like some sort of fever dream at this point. The maternity ward is a world in which so many things that are utterly abnormal in everyday life are completely normalized. Like cutting open people and pulling tiny humans out of them, for one. It's a bit odd when you think how incredibly momentous this moment is for all of the parents in the ward, and yet how it is just another day on the job for the nurses, doctors and staff. We were cheap and opted for the free post-birth room, which meant that it was shared with three other new parents, separated by curtains. I actually liked this. I felt like I was getting to share in a wide array of experiences, and the extra commotion and noise meant nothing to the kids, and also nothing to Donna and I since we could have slept at any moment. Which is a tip too - if you are having an induced delivery have it in the MORNING. We did it the opposite way, where we started at 8pm when Donna's water broke while eating pizza at my friend Dave's house. This meant that we delivered at around 5am, and then had missed an entire night of sleep to start our parenting experience.

So that gets us to month zero. I will write about the first 18 months post-arrival in the format of an extended list of pros and cons.

### The Cons
1. **Early Days Fatigue**

The early days are intense. If you weren't sleep deprived they really might not be so trying, but we both were (as per the bad timing mentioned above). 

I think the fatigue serves to slightly amplify other negative or nervous emotions you may be having. I clearly remember walking out of the hospital doors carrying these two tiny infants and being slightly surprised that no one had stopped me. You are giving me a life and death job and I have zero experience? Did they not check my CV? I felt a strong sense of imposter's syndrome descend as I ordered an Uber to come pick us up. Was I allowed to do that with newborns? Was there another option? It's disorienting because you don't really have any idea what is normal and what's not, and you don't want to get it wrong. 

As the sleep deprivation mounted - once you get home the kid(s) need to be fed every three hours, and the feeding process itself can easily take up to an hour - it actually became a bit helpful in terms of taking the edge off this worry about whether you were doing things correctly. "Do we really need to boil the bottles after every meal?"", you wonder. Tired you assures you that you definitely do not. It will be fine. 

But the sleep deprivation effects are definitely not all positive. It made me pretty grumpy at times. I had a short fuse. I got into arguments with Donna. I got into arguments with Mom while she was visiting. I occasionally lost my patience with the kids and would talk to them in what would definitely not be considered a fatherly tone. And then I would feel the stress not just of the situation and the fatigue but also the meta-awareness that I was doing this thing wrong - that this was a wonderful special time and here I was being frustrated and grumpy.

2. **Communication Barrier**

This was and is a pretty big one for me. It is frustrating when you genuinely want to help someone who seems unhappy and you **just don't know** what in the world they are unhappy about. This was, thankfully, not that common in the early days, but there were definitely moments of inconsolable crying where you try everything you can think of and nothing works.

### The Pros
Both in terms of aspects I enjoyed as well as things that we did (occasionally unwittingly) that ended up being valuable.

- early day bizarro time - watching french tv shows...3am hand offs
- scheduling
- pumping
- vip treatment
- sleep training
- reframes your view of life
- the time off with the kids is kind of a reset after they are born...you become so tired and your routine is so disrupted that you look at things more freshly...why did I think I needed to do X anyway before?
- interesting...physiologically at first...then in other ways...mentally...what is going on??
- surprising
- a lot more laughter than before
- forced me to embrace organization...
