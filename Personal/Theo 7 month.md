Dear Theo,

Seven months. Well, seven months and 11 days at this point. I started a draft of a letter the day before your birthday but rereading it now I decided to start afresh. A large part of this is simply the fact that you have changed enough in the past 11 days that my observations already felt out of date. 

Currently you are sleeping in the Baby Bjorn while nestled on your mom's chest, while she is sleeping nestled into her weird but apparently effective neck cushion. We are flying from Vancouver to Toronto, which came as a bit of a surprise to us seeing as we had a flight from Vancouver to Halifax and were not informed of the change until we arrived at the airport and were told our flight, quote, did not exist.

You took this news, it must be said, very well, and definitely much better than I. You saw it as an opportunity to practice your climbing skills on the baggage scale - which served the dual purpose of jungle gym apparatus and indicator that you had cracked the seven kilogram barrier. This means that you have more than doubled your weight. Your brother and sister also weighed themselves and they weighed almost exactly double of what you weigh. The check in agent next to us was extremely impressed by your determined clambering. He spent, no joke, about 10 minutes just observing you and complimenting you and trying to get you to smile. And he had no kids. And he was not even our check in agent. And there was a line of dozens of people waiting to check in. 

After check in we went to a lounge for some dinner and you devoured everything your mom put near your mouth. Apple. Potato salad. Basil tomato bisque. You are probably the least picky eater in the family at this point. Or perhaps least discerning. What was amazing to me was that even 4 hours past your bedtime you were bright eyed and bouncy. Your mom said, while sitting with you on the plane pre-takeoff - as you bounced up and down delightedly in her lap - that it was like you were on drugs.

This is what we often say about someone who seems to be acting outside the bounds of what we deem normal behavior. "What are you on", we might say to a friend who is acting a bit kooky. The idea is that behavior of that sort would require some sort of mind altering substance to be involved. But in your case your kookiness is all you. Your mind altering substance is the emerging Theodoros Caribou Jack Wilson. Each neuron becoming more your own and tuned to your wavelength. And what a wavelength it is.

It is a wavelength full of tenacity and aspiration. A wavelength that saw you, while waiting for our flight, bonk your head on the floor hard enough that people in an approximate 10 meter radius heard and displayed a collective look of concern as you fell of the luggage you were clambering on. And once you had been given a hug and calmed down you immediately did the exact same thing again. 

You seem genuinely entertained by your brother - who can see this and it eggs him on.

And just generally you want to be involved. Close to the action. Part of the action.

In the lounge you were chasing around the cart of the lady clearing plates from tables (she loved this).

You want to stand up. 

You get bored. You want to go outside and eat dirt.

You're willing to put anything in your mouth.

You are easy to put in a good mood but hard to make actually laugh. A sophisticated audience.

You seem so different than your siblings already.
The idea of how much of your existence is luck.
Your differences are luck.
Who you are is luck.
Where you will live is luck.

But once you toss the die you have a number. So at a certain point luck stops or no longer applies. You are you. It is not luck anymore.
