I find myself often thinking back to the vlogs I watched about a month ago by early 20s people with CAS. It makes me think a few things:
- why did it take me over 3 and a half years to watch this? I have known about that fact that Amos probably has CAS since he was just over 2. 
- are these best case scenario CAS kids?
- when they say their parents really advocated for them how does that compare to what I am doing and will do? will Amos think that I "really" advocated for him?
- does advocating involve more than trying to make the best of a given situation for him? Should the goal be not to make the best of a situation chosen for Donna/me but rather to find the best possible situation for him?
- What will bullying look like for him? 
- Is his condition going to push him to get really into some sort of activity/interest as an escape?
- Does home schooling make sense in order to avoid bullying until he is old enough to deal with it?
- Does bullying have lasting effects?
- If height is positively correlated with salary/status how negatively correlated is CAS?
- Does Amos think that he is talking? Both vloggers mentioned that they thought they were talking comprehensibly when they were kids and just making nonsense sounds.
- Are we doing enough for Amos to get him to where the vloggers ended up? 
- Would Amos benefit from more SLP/practice or is it just a developmental thing where it is like trying to stretch someone out so they grow? Though are we sure that does not work if you do it enough?
- How were the vloggers at sports?

Just in the same way that the smallest thing can send him into fits of laughter, does the smallest effort elicit a feeling of extreme fatigue? # Friday, March 3, 2023
Amos was sad going to school again today. All week he has been quite resistant. I would really appreciate a Korean private school style spy-cam. I think it would help to know how his days unfold to know how to help him learn coping skills. 

The level of frustration he expresses seems to be ramping up. He has different ways of expressing it. One is a short sharp yell/bark into the face of the person he is feeling frustrated with. Usually someone who is doing something he doesn't like. Such as Rilka, who this morning, was playing on his bed. He initially wanted her to lay down with him on the bed but then when she didn't want to do that he wanted her off the bed. She refused and he yelled in her face. He will lean in to get close, yell/bark, and then lean back looking mad. The other common frustration display is an escalating whine/cry/shout combo that also often involves body movements like arm flapping, or a hand gesture like he is pushing something/someone away. 

I feel like there are a few storms on the horizon approaching and I am not sure which is going to hit first and which is going to be most difficult to deal with. And I am not even sure how to dress appropriately for these novel weather phenomenon.

The frustration is definitely one of those storms. As the complexity and nuance of Amos' wants/needs continue to increase at rate x, his communication skills are increasing at a fraction of that rate, meaning that the gap between the two is growing. This creates both more opportunities for frustration and higher magnitude frustrations. We already have reports of Amos grabbing another kids hair and not letting go. Part of the reason that this made me so sad is I had to imagine Amos being so upset that he would do something like that. This is not a kid, or at least seems not to be a kid, who gets perverse pleasure in hurting others. He is largely kind and warm and loving. So for him to be so mean tells me (and of course this could be at least partly the rationalizations of a parent) that Amos must have been REALLY upset. Again, this is why I want spy-cam. What made him upset? How can I help him navigate that sort of upset-ness? And what kind of a monster is this kid who made him so upset? I don't really mean that. But of course a small part of me thinks it.

This seems like one version of the approaching frustration storm. Lashing out. Trying to hurt the source of the frustration. There are other versions. None seem good. The most obvious - besides the first option - is that of stepping back. Of avoidance and isolation. If being with other kids is frustrating then avoid being with other kids. I do feel very grateful, in this instance, for the fact that he has Rilka around. He really can't avoid being around her, so navigating that will be non-optional. There is also the option of becoming very passive. Of not avoiding other kids but of just letting them have their way. This seems like an unlikely route.

A subset of this storm is also connected to another that I see on the horizon. Specifically, getting made fun of. Obviously this is one experience that can lead to frustration, but it is so much more than that. This is perhaps the storm that I find myself most trying to look away from. Both vloggers with CAS talked about getting made fun of as kids. Granted one actually said that she didn't really get made fun of that much - however she also pointed out that she spent multiple years being completely silent and avoiding most encounters with others. Which seems a pretty high price to pay to avoid getting made fun of. Also I think that girls probably get made fun of slightly less than boys. Girls have their own toxic ways of establishing the pecking order but it seems like there is less focus on people with clear disabilities. Boys however tend to - at least in my experience - have no such compunctions.

I imagine it starting with curiosity. Well, I don't have to imagine. I have already seen it happening. At Theo pick up one evening the 4 or 5 year old daughter of a Brazilian dad was interacting with Amos and noticed that he was not responding to her comments/questions. She asked him to say "maman" and when he didn't she asked again and then asked me if he could say "maman". I said that he could (since it is actually one of his less than 10 words).

- uh-oh
- wow
- mama
- papa
- more
- me
- moi
- no (getting close)

Amos' sounds:
- ah

- ee (though not perfect)
- m
- p
- la-la
