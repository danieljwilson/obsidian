# La Saladita
We spent the better part of a week in La Saladita, arriving Monday around noon and leaving the following Saturday at 5:30am. A few observations and thoughts from that time, both for my own personal memory and also as a pseudo-guide in case anyone who is taking a trip to La Saladita asks me for recommendations.

The format is a running diary of our time there (us being myself and Donna), followed by some general thoughts.

## Monday
We flew into the Ixtapa/Zihuatenejo airport from Mexico City having flown into Mexico City pretty late the night before - arriving at our hotel close to midnight and then having to leave at 7:45am for our 9:55am departure. We got to the airport in plenty of time and saw that MEX was rife with lounge options through either my Amex card or Priority Pass and set out to try a few. However, we started with the Centurion lounge which was more like a restaurant where you had table service and a menu, and, honestly, really good food options. So we basically spent our whole time there. It was an interesting model as while all the food and drinks were free, the service was not, so you just had to leave a tip. The service was very good.

Flight was about an hour and uneventful.

The arrival airport was tiny which meant that we were from plane to outside ready for pick up in about 5 minutes. Donna had organized a driver through the host of the house we were staying at in La Saladita. When he arrived he handed some money to the official taxi drivers milling about the entrance and then we went out to his car and headed first to a grocery store, to stock up, and then to La Saladita, about 45 minutes away.

We had to wait to check in until about 1:30pm so went for lunch at Benny's, one of the many beachfront restaurants. I ordered beans and rice and donna had a fruit shake. Both were really great. The prices were not crazy but were definitely significantly higher that what I expected. $8.50 CAD for beans and rice and the same for the shake. It quickly became clear that the whole beach had gringo-ified their prices. Which is normal, it was full of gringos.

We then got settled in our house, called Casa Nu, which was pretty great. It was a boardform concrete construction, kind of in the tiny home style, with an efficient layout consisting of a lofted single bedroom up above and a living/dining/bathroom/shower area below. It was surrounded by mango trees which was great for two reasons. First they provided much appreciated shade, given that the days were all in the 32-34 degree range, and also every few hours you would here a small thud as a ripe mango fell off one of the trees. They were delicious and used in shakes, oatmeal and just eaten straight up.

Tuesday/Wednesday/Thursday/Friday

Other than the fact that Donna did a surfing lesson on the Tuesday, these days were all pretty much the same. They generally involved:
- morning and/or evening jog
- swimming/body-surfing
- a couple hours of pseudo "work"
- lunch at the house
- napping
- surfing
- dinner
- movie (part of a)
- bed

La Saladita Takeaways
- Really nice wave. I really love point breaks as I really don't love having to smash through a bunch of waves trying to get out. It was not as consistent in my experience as Scorpion Bay (Baja California) but was between 3 and 7 feet the whole time we were there with waves that you could ride for 30+ seconds.
- People were friendly. Both in the lineup and on land/sand. Definitely a lot of Americans and Canadians. Definitely a lot of beginner surfers. Also a lot of older people with pretty even representation of 20s through 50s. We talked to a couple that had just purchased a property and closed on it that week. They said the agent they used had said that it was going to be the next Troncones (a nearby surf town). Apparently houses there like the one they got are $1 million USD. Best of luck to them.
- Unsurprisingly, things have changed quite a bit in the 25 years since I first started going to Mexico. Just a bit of a vibe-shift where when I was traveling before it felt like the other foreigners you met were pretty adventurous - either hard core backpackers or surfers. Whereas now it seems like there is a much broader and wealthier cross section with a lot more "entrepreneur"/digital nomad representation.
- The restaurants all seemed to serve basically the same thing so we just settled on one (Sergio's) and ate the same thing for dinner every night - a veggie burrito with an order of chips and salsa (the homemade salsa was simple but fantastic). The burrito was 100 pesos and the chips and salsa 50 pesos. Beers were 35 pesos which was the cheapest we found on the beach.
- I rented my surfboards from Chucho's as it was the cheapest (300 pesos for 24 hours). They were pretty laid back, both naturally and substance-aided. When I broke a fin and asked how much to fix it they just shrugged and said I could get them a beer. The surfboards were of pretty mediocre quality, but fine for a beginner like me.
- There was a little convenience store on the entrance to the town that had all the basic staples and wasn't that much more expensive than the mega-mart we had shopped at in Ixtapa.
- It was nice to have air conditioning in our bedroom for sleeping purposes.

Mexico City

Saturday
Got in early in the morning and went back to the same hotel we had stayed at previously, Hotel MX Roma, as the location is great, the rooms are fine, and the prices very reasonable ($190 CAD for two nights). We dropped our bags off and started our day.
- Rosetta: We had been recommended this bakery by multiple people and given that it was two blocks away went to check it out. It was an hour wait to get a table so we just went in the take out line as you could get all menu items for takeaway. There was a sizable line but it moved quickly. I got the chilaquiles and a pastry and their carrot/ginger juice. We took our food to the Rio de Janeiro park and watched the dogs splash and play in the fountain while we ate on a bench. It was great.
- Museo Tamayo: We got there just after opening time which was nice as it was largely empty. There was a really nice exhibition of ARTIST NAME whose work I was not familiar with. A lot of the pieces involved active interventions like one where there were piles of Persian rugs and multiple times of weeks a pack of dogs would be taken into the museum and would play dead on the rugs. It's a nice size of museum, within an hour to 90 minutes you can feel like you have gotten a decent overview of the exhibits, which is about how long I can engage anyway.
- Bosque Chapultepec: The museum is in the city's major park which is full of vendors which we walked through on our way to the park exit (though not before buying luchador masks for the kids). 
- Kurimanzutto+: We started at Kurimanzutto which had a nice exhibition of Lilia Carrillo, and then walked to a few other galleries in the area, topping things off with some amazing paletas. 
- Museum: We walked through Condesa on our way back to the hotel, stopping first at the Condesa DF hotel to see if we could have a drink on their rooftop patio (private event, no dice), and then at the MUSEUM NAME, which was both free and interesting.
- TACO PLACE: Had the 5 al pastor taquito special (85 pesos) at the vegan TACO PLACE NAME, along with a green juice. Recommend.
- Running in PARK: After a nap we went for an evening run once the heat broke, taking an Uber over to the PARK and then running loops. 
- TACO PLACE: For our second vegan al pastor taco experience of the day we went to TACO PLACE, where there was a 75 peso special for 4 tacos. There was no seating per se, but we sat on doorstep which was fine. The tacos were great, better than the previous ones we had had, and there were lots of good toppings including nopale (cactus) which I really like. While there a guy from San Diego also ordered from the stand and told us that this was the best tacos in the city.
- La Clandestina: I had been here close to a decade ago and boy have the vibes changed. At the time it felt like a slightly insider place and now it feels like its focus is tourists. And the prices are pretty high. It started at 90 pesos for an ounce. And, honestly, I didn't really like it very much (I tried two different kinds). 
- Marcello: After walking back toward our hotel we made two more stops, the first here, which was fine but not special. Lots of people drinking Aperol Spritzs.
- Bar Felix: This was our last stop and another place I had fond memories of from the days when I was doing an artist residency in Mexico City a decade ago. The service and location was the same but definitely the crowd had changed. Or maybe I had changed? But it definitely seemed notably less "cool" and the dancing, once it started, was much worse.

Sunday
- Reforma/Bosque Chapultepec: We got up relatively early (8am) to go for a long run and try and beat the heat. We started on Reforma by the Angel of Independence as they close down the street each Sunday for walkers/joggers/cyclists. It was great - a ton of people out running. 
- Rosetta: After the run did a repeat of Saturday with a take out breakfast from Rosetta - this time fruit, yogourt and granola and fresh squeezed juice. Also got some fig marmalade and cookies to take home.
- Pantera: Got a couple of paletas before heading back to the hotel. Donna strawberry, me guayaba. Both good but not as good as the previous iterations.
- Hotel MX Roma: Nap time (and a bit of work for me)
- Lagunilla Market: I was looking for some presents and to check out the weekly antique market so we headed over. At first we were in the daily market area but then got redirected to the actual antique market. It was great. Got some crickets/grasshoppers? to take back. Also had a couple of very large Micheladas (which everyone seemed to be drinking). Lots of fund stuff to look through.
- Contramar: Another place with multiple recommendations. Also a score of 4.? on Google with XX,XXX reviews. We didn't have a reservation but put ourselves on the list and had a table within an hour. Just as we got inside it started to rain like crazy after some loud thunder/lightning. The meal was great. Very fast and friendly service. Had ceviche and two types of fish tacos and all was great. Also a fig tart for desert. Highly recommended.

Monday
Alarm set for 4:50am and immediately we tried to get an Uber given the often lengthy wait times even during the day. It timed out on an Uber X but finally we managed to snag an Uber comfort that was going to take 11 minutes to arrive. It came at about 5:12 and took 18 minutes to get to the airport.

Flying out we had to check a bag since I had purchased some liquid-esque (marmalade, salsa) items. This turned out to be helpful as I had actually lost my little immigration slip that I had received on the way in when you do the automated immigration process, which, admittedly, had clearly on the top of the slip: "Do not lose. Keep for your exit." I had ignored this directive and lost the slip. When checking my bag, however, the agent asked to see my passport and realized I had neither a stamp in my passport or the requisite slip. I admitted to her that I had forgotten it and she did a sort of vocal eye roll sigh combination and then printed out a boarding pass for me on which she stamped "Papers OK" in red ink. I'm not sure what they would have done had I tried to go through security without that stamp on my passport but surely it would have been more annoying than the nothing that they did given that I did have the stamp.

Security was maybe a 10 minute wait and then we went to our gate. I had been planning to go to the Centurion lounge for international flights but when I went to the location it said it was pre-security which was not only non-ideal for us but just seems like a bad idea in general as it makes timing your stay in the lounge really hard given that it isn't really possible to know with exactitude the time you need to pass through security. So we ended up going back to the domestic Centurion lounge which was great. Our waiter kept upselling us to more free (and very good) items, including for me a green juice, a hot chocolate, and a pain au chocolat.
