#### Never let a good crisis go to waste.
A crisis, by definition, throws normal routines and mindsets completely out of whack. The  breaking of a routine or mental framework can be uncomfortable, unpleasant, and disconcerting. But it also allows, or even forces, consideration of alternatives.

#### Re-evaluation.
It is easy to live a minimally examined life. Especially if it is going well - and perhaps it is even the result of some thoughtful and well-conducted previous examination at an earlier point in our lives. But we are changing. Just as it is fruitful to re-examine the same phenomena in the natural sciences every time we develop a new tool with higher resolution, it can be useful to use the latest, most intelligent and experienced version of ourselves to re-examine our lives and our choices.

#### You will be okay.
This is not to say that you are okay, or that there are not better and worse paths laid out before you, but you, being you, will be okay. No matter which path you choose.

#### It might be hard to know the correct answer in advance.
Sometimes we just have to choose. We should, of course, do our best but given that we do not have full information we will, inevitably, often be wrong. That is fine, as long as we make sure that we:
1. Lean into the choice and give it a good faith best effort
2. Are also willing to go back to the decision point and make a completely different choice

#### Honesty.
Pulling back from a choice we have committed to is incredibly hard. But shoehorning ourselves into an ill-fitting choice is a recipe for discomfort. And sometimes a choice that was originally correct can turn into a bad fit. And vice versa. We change. Being honest with ourselves, and about ourselves with others, may push us into a position where we have to acknowledge that a choice or situation is not working. But as with most things, making changes, even hard changes, becomes easier with practice. Sometimes things are just hard, but the tricky thing is distinguishing between the productive discomfort of a challenge and the corrosive discomfort of an existential bad fit.

#### Remember what you have within you.
I've pointed this out before but it bears repeating. You are an incredible person. I agree that everyone is unique, but the spectrum of that uniqueness is, generally, and this is just my own personal opinion, fairly narrow. You, however, are quite far over on the uniqueness distribution. This perhaps means that the contradictions that are present in all of us are  especially strong in you. I think that this can be challenging at times to live with, but it is also exactly that Hudspithean dialectic that is the fuel of your brilliance. As I pointed out before, you will be okay. And your version of okay may be unusual. Or it may not. But if it is an honest expression of your character it will be a good choice.

I think that when we hear of someone who is in a difficult situation there are at least a few standard reactions. The first of those, generally, is that of sympathy. Often that is followed by an attempt to draw a connection between one's own lived experience and that of the sympathetic object. This seems, in many cases, to not go very well. If the person in question has just received a terrible medical diagnosis it may not be altogether useful to learn that the interlocutor's grandmother happened to die of that very same ailment. Or even that they heard of someone with the condition in question who made a miraculous recovery. Even if the statement is coming from a good and well-meaning place, which is usually the case, it generally falls painfully flat and in doing so does not just not improve things, but actively makes them worse by sprinkling a dose of annoyance and possibly even anger to an already fraught situation.

So now I am going to, naturally, tell you about an experience that I had which reminds me in some tangential way of your own.

When we were pregnant and deciding what our parental leave was going to look like we had a number of conversations where I was pushing to spend that time outside of Canada and my first suggestion was that we go to Mexico. We could live on a beach, eat well, hire very affordable childcare, enjoy tropical weather and just generally live a higher quality of life for a lower price than might be available in Toronto.

Donna didn't want to do it. I pushed her trying to get her to express what the problem was with Mexico. She cited violence/safety, medical care, and the fact that she didn't speak Spanish very well. Even though I didn't really agree with these critiques I felt that they were rational concerns, and that if I wanted to insist on Mexico that I would have to address these concerns. In this case I never did that as France was a close second place and didn't have those same issues in Donna's mind. 

However, my point is that I think that in any situation in which you have a partner that doesn't agree with a plan that involves them the onus falls to the plan-proposer to address those concerns. Acknowledging at the outset of this process that: (1) this may be a lot of work, (2) you may not agree with their concerns in the first place, and (3) even if you feel that you have addressed the concerns adequately the other person may not come around, and that in this case either you drop the plan or you push through realizing and accepting that there may well be consequences.

That said, I think it is reasonable in a relationship to have a threshold of articulation and expression in order for emotional needs to be met. If you say that you are okay with something I think it is reasonable for someone to assume that you mean what you say. It is of course very nice and appreciated if someone is able to read beyond your words and pick up on your unexpressed feelings, or unconscious communication, but this is a pretty high bar, and one that is particularly hard to clear if the other person is distracted.

So this seems like your work Dave. Expressing your true feelings clearly and without fear.

But of course this is not all on you. And this isn't something that just "happened". Choices were made and actions were taken. There may well be underlying psychological needs that drove these choices and actions, but if you are in a relationship and the only way you have of dealing with a psychological need is unhealthy to the relationship, then you need to invest time and effort into finding an alternative solution. If you had anger issues and found that you needed to get black out drunk and get in bar fights a couple of times a year as a release or else fall into a critical psychological state, then it is a reasonable demand by your partner that you find an alternate path to satisfy this psychological need (while acknowledging that the need is real).
