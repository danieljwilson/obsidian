# Claude
**First, figure out what you're paying at Fidelity.** Actively managed 401k accounts often charge 0.5%–1.5% in advisory fees _on top of_ the underlying fund expense ratios. On $200k+, even 1% means $2,000+/year. Log into Fidelity and look for an "fees" or "account summary" section, or call them directly. If you're paying north of 0.5% all-in for management, that's worth addressing since it compounds significantly over 25 years.

**The Schwab brokerage sitting unmanaged is worth auditing.** If it was actively managed and now isn't, you may be holding a scattered collection of positions that no longer reflect a coherent strategy. The question is whether to consolidate, rebalance in place, or do a gradual transition — and the answer depends on embedded capital gains. Selling everything at once could trigger a big tax bill. A tax-aware transition over 2–3 years (harvesting losses, letting gains defer) is often the smarter move.

**For your low-maintenance, low-fee, aggressive approach, here's the general playbook:**

For tax-advantaged accounts (SEP IRA, 401k), you have the most flexibility since there are no tax consequences for rebalancing. A simple aggressive allocation might be something like 80–90% total US stock market index and 10–20% international stock index, all in low-cost index funds (Fidelity Zero funds, Schwab total market funds, or Vanguard equivalents have expense ratios near 0%). You could simplify even further with a single target-date fund if your 401k offers a good one with low fees.

For taxable brokerage accounts (Schwab, Vanguard), tax efficiency matters more. Broad index ETFs like VTI, VXUS, or their Schwab equivalents (SCHB, SCHF) are naturally tax-efficient because they have low turnover and distribute fewer capital gains. You'd want to avoid holding bonds or high-dividend funds here — those generate ordinary income taxed at higher rates. If you hold bonds at all given your aggressive stance, keep them in the SEP IRA or 401k.

**A few S-Corp-specific considerations:**

Your $500/month salary is quite low. The IRS expects S-Corp owners to pay themselves "reasonable compensation," and if you're audited, an unreasonably low salary could be recharacterized, triggering back payroll taxes plus penalties. This is worth discussing with your CPA, especially given that you work in a well-compensated field. Your SEP IRA contribution limit is also based on your W-2 salary from the S-Corp, so a $6,000/year salary severely limits what you can contribute there (roughly 25% of compensation, so ~$1,500). If your 401k is also through the S-Corp, similar contribution limits apply based on salary. Structuring your compensation properly could unlock significantly more tax-advantaged retirement savings.

**Possible simplified structure going forward:**

You could consolidate toward fewer accounts — for example, keep Vanguard as your taxable brokerage (since the robo advisor is working for you), roll the Schwab brokerage holdings into Vanguard over time as tax-efficient opportunities arise, keep the SEP IRA at Schwab or roll it into the 401k if Fidelity allows it, and either take control of the 401k yourself with index funds or move it if Fidelity's plan fees are high. Fewer accounts means less maintenance and easier rebalancing.

**Bottom line:** The biggest immediate wins are probably finding out your Fidelity fees, auditing the orphaned Schwab positions, and talking to your CPA about whether your salary level exposes you to risk and whether you're leaving retirement contribution room on the table. The investment side — shifting to index funds — is relatively straightforward once you know the tax implications of transitioning.
